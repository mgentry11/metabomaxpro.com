Question 16.117
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
Professor Leonard: Jacobian & Change of Variables 3Blue1Brown: The Determinant

(a) Find a linear transformation taking the square \(S = [0,1] \times [0,1]\) to the parallelogram \(P\) with vertices \((0,0), (2,0), (1,2), (3,2)\).

(b) Write down a change of variables formula appropriate to the transformation you found in part (a).

Solution
Part (a): Finding the Linear Transformation
Key Concept: Linear Transformations

A linear transformation from \((u,v)\) to \((x,y)\) has the form:

\[T(u,v) = (au + bv, cu + dv)\]

We find the coefficients by seeing where the corners of the unit square map to.

Step 1: Identify the corner mappings

The unit square has corners at \((0,0), (1,0), (0,1), (1,1)\).

The parallelogram has vertices at \((0,0), (2,0), (1,2), (3,2)\).

We need to match them up:

  • \((0,0) \to (0,0)\) (origin to origin)
  • \((1,0) \to (2,0)\) (right edge of square to one side of parallelogram)
  • \((0,1) \to (1,2)\) (top edge of square to other side of parallelogram)
  • \((1,1) \to (3,2)\) (this should work automatically if the others are correct)
Step 2: Find the coefficients

From \(T(u,v) = (au + bv, cu + dv)\):

  • \(T(1,0) = (a, c) = (2, 0)\) â†’ so \(a = 2\) and \(c = 0\)
  • \(T(0,1) = (b, d) = (1, 2)\) â†’ so \(b = 1\) and \(d = 2\)
Step 3: Verify with the fourth corner

\(T(1,1) = (2(1) + 1(1), 0(1) + 2(1)) = (3, 2)\) âœ“

Answer (a)
\[T(u,v) = (2u + v, 2v)\]

Or equivalently: \(x = 2u + v\), \(y = 2v\)

Part (b): Change of Variables Formula
Key Concept: The Jacobian

When changing variables, we need the Jacobian determinant:

\[\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix}\]
Step 1: Compute the partial derivatives

From \(x = 2u + v\) and \(y = 2v\):

\[\frac{\partial x}{\partial u} = 2, \quad \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} = 1, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} = 0, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} = 2\]
Step 2: Calculate the Jacobian
\[\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{vmatrix} = (2)(2) - (1)(0) = 4\]
Answer (b)
\[\iint_P f(x,y) \, dA = \iint_S f(2u+v, 2v) \cdot 4 \, du \, dv\]

where \(S = [0,1] \times [0,1]\)

Question 16.118
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
Professor Leonard: Jacobian & Change of Variables 3Blue1Brown: The Determinant

(a) Find the image of the square \([0,1] \times [0,1]\) under the transformation \(T(x,y) = (2x, x + 3y)\).

(b) Write down a change of variables formula appropriate to the transformation and the region you found in part (a).

Solution
Part (a): Finding the Image
Step 1: Transform each corner of the square

Let \(u = 2x\) and \(v = x + 3y\). Apply this to each corner:

  • \((0,0) \to (2(0), 0 + 3(0)) = (0, 0)\)
  • \((1,0) \to (2(1), 1 + 3(0)) = (2, 1)\)
  • \((0,1) \to (2(0), 0 + 3(1)) = (0, 3)\)
  • \((1,1) \to (2(1), 1 + 3(1)) = (2, 4)\)
Step 2: Identify the shape

Plotting these points: \((0,0), (2,1), (0,3), (2,4)\)

These form a parallelogram (linear transformations always map parallelograms to parallelograms).

Answer (a)

The image is a parallelogram with vertices at \((0,0), (2,1), (0,3), (2,4)\)

Part (b): Change of Variables Formula
Step 1: Compute the Jacobian

With \(u = 2x\) and \(v = x + 3y\):

\[\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \\ \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{vmatrix} = 6\]
Answer (b)
\[\iint_P f(u,v) \, du \, dv = \iint_S f(2x, x+3y) \cdot 6 \, dx \, dy\]

where \(S = [0,1] \times [0,1]\) and \(P\) is the parallelogram with vertices \((0,0), (2,1), (0,3), (2,4)\)

Question 16.119
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
Professor Leonard: Jacobian & Change of Variables

Let \(B\) be the region in the first quadrant bounded by the curves \(xy = 1\), \(xy = 3\), \(x^2 - y^2 = 1\), and \(x^2 - y^2 = 4\). Evaluate

\[\iint_B (x^2 + y^2) \, dx \, dy\]

using the change of variables \(u = x^2 - y^2\), \(v = xy\).

Solution
Key Insight

The given substitution transforms the curved boundaries into straight lines! With \(u = x^2 - y^2\) and \(v = xy\):

  • \(xy = 1\) becomes \(v = 1\)
  • \(xy = 3\) becomes \(v = 3\)
  • \(x^2 - y^2 = 1\) becomes \(u = 1\)
  • \(x^2 - y^2 = 4\) becomes \(u = 4\)

So \(B\) transforms to the rectangle \([1,4] \times [1,3]\) in the \(uv\)-plane!

Step 1: Find the Jacobian \(\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}\)
\[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 2x, \quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -2y\] \[\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = y, \quad \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = x\] \[\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} = \begin{vmatrix} 2x & -2y \\ y & x \end{vmatrix} = 2x^2 + 2y^2 = 2(x^2 + y^2)\]
Step 2: Find the inverse Jacobian

We need \(\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\) for the change of variables:

\[\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right| = \frac{1}{\left|\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}\right|} = \frac{1}{2(x^2 + y^2)}\]
Step 3: Transform the integral
\[\iint_B (x^2 + y^2) \, dx \, dy = \iint_{B'} (x^2 + y^2) \cdot \frac{1}{2(x^2 + y^2)} \, du \, dv\] \[= \iint_{B'} \frac{1}{2} \, du \, dv\]

where \(B' = [1,4] \times [1,3]\)

Step 4: Evaluate
\[= \frac{1}{2} \int_1^4 \int_1^3 dv \, du = \frac{1}{2} \cdot (4-1) \cdot (3-1) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 3 \cdot 2 = 3\]
Answer
\[\iint_B (x^2 + y^2) \, dx \, dy = 3\]
Question 16.121
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
Professor Leonard: Triple Integrals (Cylindrical/Spherical)

Find the volume inside the surfaces \(x^2 + y^2 = z\) and \(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 2\).

Solution
Understanding the Surfaces
  • \(x^2 + y^2 = z\) is a paraboloid opening upward with vertex at origin
  • \(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 2\) is a sphere of radius \(\sqrt{2}\) centered at origin

We want the volume of the region inside the sphere and above the paraboloid.

Step 1: Use cylindrical coordinates

Let \(x = r\cos\theta\), \(y = r\sin\theta\), \(z = z\).

  • Paraboloid: \(r^2 = z\)
  • Sphere: \(r^2 + z^2 = 2\)
Step 2: Find the intersection

Substituting \(r^2 = z\) into \(r^2 + z^2 = 2\):

\[z + z^2 = 2\] \[z^2 + z - 2 = 0\] \[(z + 2)(z - 1) = 0\]

So \(z = 1\) (taking the positive value). At this height, \(r^2 = 1\), so \(r = 1\).

Step 3: Set up the integral

For \(0 \le r \le 1\): \(z\) goes from the paraboloid (\(z = r^2\)) up to the sphere (\(z = \sqrt{2 - r^2}\))

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^1 \int_{r^2}^{\sqrt{2-r^2}} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta\]
Step 4: Evaluate the z-integral
\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^1 r\left[\sqrt{2-r^2} - r^2\right] dr \, d\theta\] \[= 2\pi \int_0^1 \left(r\sqrt{2-r^2} - r^3\right) dr\]
Step 5: Evaluate each part

For \(\int_0^1 r\sqrt{2-r^2} \, dr\): Let \(u = 2 - r^2\), \(du = -2r \, dr\)

\[= -\frac{1}{2}\int_2^1 \sqrt{u} \, du = \frac{1}{2}\int_1^2 \sqrt{u} \, du = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{2}{3}\left[u^{3/2}\right]_1^2 = \frac{1}{3}(2\sqrt{2} - 1)\]

For \(\int_0^1 r^3 \, dr = \frac{1}{4}\)

Step 6: Combine
\[V = 2\pi\left[\frac{1}{3}(2\sqrt{2} - 1) - \frac{1}{4}\right]\] \[= 2\pi\left[\frac{2\sqrt{2} - 1}{3} - \frac{1}{4}\right]\] \[= 2\pi\left[\frac{4(2\sqrt{2} - 1) - 3}{12}\right]\] \[= 2\pi\left[\frac{8\sqrt{2} - 7}{12}\right]\] \[= \frac{\pi(8\sqrt{2} - 7)}{6}\]
Answer
\[V = \frac{\pi(8\sqrt{2} - 7)}{6}\]
Question 16.122
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
Professor Leonard: Triple Integrals (Cylindrical/Spherical)

Find the volume enclosed by the cone \(x^2 + y^2 = z^2\) and the plane \(2z - y - 2 = 0\).

Solution
Understanding the Geometry
  • The cone \(x^2 + y^2 = z^2\) has two nappes. We'll use \(z = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\) (upper nappe).
  • The plane \(2z - y - 2 = 0\) can be rewritten as \(z = \frac{y + 2}{2}\)
Step 1: Find the curve of intersection

Setting \(\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = \frac{y + 2}{2}\):

\[x^2 + y^2 = \frac{(y+2)^2}{4}\] \[4x^2 + 4y^2 = y^2 + 4y + 4\] \[4x^2 + 3y^2 - 4y = 4\] \[4x^2 + 3\left(y - \frac{2}{3}\right)^2 = 4 + \frac{4}{3} = \frac{16}{3}\]

This is an ellipse in the xy-plane.

Step 2: Set up in cylindrical coordinates

Using \(x = r\cos\theta\), \(y = r\sin\theta\):

  • Cone: \(z = r\)
  • Plane: \(z = \frac{r\sin\theta + 2}{2}\)

The region is bounded below by the cone and above by the plane.

Step 3: Find the boundary curve in polar

At the intersection: \(r = \frac{r\sin\theta + 2}{2}\)

\[2r = r\sin\theta + 2\] \[r(2 - \sin\theta) = 2\] \[r = \frac{2}{2 - \sin\theta}\]
Step 4: Set up and evaluate the integral
\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\frac{2}{2-\sin\theta}} \int_r^{\frac{r\sin\theta + 2}{2}} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta\] \[= \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\frac{2}{2-\sin\theta}} r\left(\frac{r\sin\theta + 2}{2} - r\right) dr \, d\theta\] \[= \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\frac{2}{2-\sin\theta}} r\left(\frac{r\sin\theta + 2 - 2r}{2}\right) dr \, d\theta\] \[= \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\frac{2}{2-\sin\theta}} \frac{r(2 - r(2-\sin\theta))}{2} dr \, d\theta\]

Let \(R = \frac{2}{2-\sin\theta}\). Then:

\[= \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{2}\left[r^2 - \frac{r^3(2-\sin\theta)}{3}\right]_0^R d\theta\] \[= \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{2}\left[R^2 - \frac{R^3(2-\sin\theta)}{3}\right] d\theta\]

Since \(R(2-\sin\theta) = 2\), we have \(R^3(2-\sin\theta) = 2R^2\):

\[= \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{2}\left[R^2 - \frac{2R^2}{3}\right] d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{R^2}{6} d\theta\] \[= \frac{1}{6}\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{4}{(2-\sin\theta)^2} d\theta = \frac{2}{3}\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{(2-\sin\theta)^2} d\theta\]
Step 5: Evaluate the trigonometric integral

Using the Weierstrass substitution \(t = \tan(\theta/2)\):

\[\sin\theta = \frac{2t}{1+t^2}, \quad d\theta = \frac{2}{1+t^2}dt\]

After detailed computation (standard integral table):

\[\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{(2-\sin\theta)^2} d\theta = \frac{4\pi}{3\sqrt{3}}\]
Answer
\[V = \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{4\pi}{3\sqrt{3}} = \frac{8\pi}{9\sqrt{3}} = \frac{8\pi\sqrt{3}}{27}\]
Question 16.123
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
Vsauce: The Napkin Ring Problem (10M+ views!) Professor Leonard: Triple Integrals (Cylindrical)

A cylindrical hole of diameter 1 is bored through a sphere of radius 2. Assuming that the axis of the cylinder passes through the center of the sphere, find the volume of the solid that remains.

Solution
Setup
  • Sphere: \(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 4\) (radius 2)
  • Cylinder: \(x^2 + y^2 = \frac{1}{4}\) (radius \(\frac{1}{2}\), diameter 1)

We want the volume of the sphere minus the cylindrical core.

Step 1: Use cylindrical coordinates

The remaining solid is the region where \(\frac{1}{2} \le r \le 2\) and \(-\sqrt{4-r^2} \le z \le \sqrt{4-r^2}\).

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_{1/2}^{2} \int_{-\sqrt{4-r^2}}^{\sqrt{4-r^2}} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta\]
Step 2: Evaluate the z-integral
\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_{1/2}^{2} 2r\sqrt{4-r^2} \, dr \, d\theta\] \[= 2\pi \cdot 2 \int_{1/2}^{2} r\sqrt{4-r^2} \, dr\] \[= 4\pi \int_{1/2}^{2} r\sqrt{4-r^2} \, dr\]
Step 3: Substitute for the r-integral

Let \(u = 4 - r^2\), so \(du = -2r \, dr\), meaning \(r \, dr = -\frac{1}{2}du\)

When \(r = \frac{1}{2}\): \(u = 4 - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{15}{4}\)

When \(r = 2\): \(u = 0\)

\[4\pi \int_{1/2}^{2} r\sqrt{4-r^2} \, dr = 4\pi \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) \int_{15/4}^{0} \sqrt{u} \, du\] \[= 2\pi \int_0^{15/4} \sqrt{u} \, du\] \[= 2\pi \left[\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\right]_0^{15/4}\] \[= \frac{4\pi}{3}\left(\frac{15}{4}\right)^{3/2}\] \[= \frac{4\pi}{3} \cdot \frac{15^{3/2}}{8}\] \[= \frac{\pi \cdot 15\sqrt{15}}{6}\] \[= \frac{15\pi\sqrt{15}}{6} = \frac{5\pi\sqrt{15}}{2}\]
Answer
\[V = \frac{5\pi\sqrt{15}}{2}\]
Question 16.124
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
Steinmetz Solid: Volume of Intersecting Cylinders Professor Leonard: Triple Integrals

Let \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) be two cylinders of infinite extent, of diameter 2, and with axes on the \(x\) and \(y\) axes, respectively. Find the volume of their intersection, \(C_1 \cap C_2\).

Solution
The Steinmetz Solid

This is a famous shape called the Steinmetz solid or bicylinder.

  • \(C_1\): axis along x-axis, so \(y^2 + z^2 \le 1\)
  • \(C_2\): axis along y-axis, so \(x^2 + z^2 \le 1\)
Step 1: Understand the cross-sections

For a fixed value of \(z\) with \(|z| \le 1\):

  • From \(C_1\): \(|y| \le \sqrt{1-z^2}\)
  • From \(C_2\): \(|x| \le \sqrt{1-z^2}\)

So the cross-section at height \(z\) is a square with side length \(2\sqrt{1-z^2}\).

Step 2: Set up the integral

The area of each square cross-section is \(\left(2\sqrt{1-z^2}\right)^2 = 4(1-z^2)\)

\[V = \int_{-1}^{1} 4(1-z^2) \, dz\]
Step 3: Evaluate
\[V = 4\int_{-1}^{1} (1-z^2) \, dz\] \[= 4\left[z - \frac{z^3}{3}\right]_{-1}^{1}\] \[= 4\left[\left(1 - \frac{1}{3}\right) - \left(-1 + \frac{1}{3}\right)\right]\] \[= 4\left[\frac{2}{3} - \left(-\frac{2}{3}\right)\right]\] \[= 4 \cdot \frac{4}{3}\] \[= \frac{16}{3}\]
Answer
\[V = \frac{16}{3}\]
Question 16.125
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
MathisPower4U: Tetrahedron Volume (Triple Integral) Professor Leonard: Triple Integrals

Find the volume bounded by \(\frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} + \frac{z}{c} = 1\) and the coordinate planes.

Solution
The Shape

This is a tetrahedron with vertices at:

  • \((0, 0, 0)\) - origin
  • \((a, 0, 0)\) - x-intercept
  • \((0, b, 0)\) - y-intercept
  • \((0, 0, c)\) - z-intercept
Step 1: Set up the triple integral

From the plane equation: \(z = c\left(1 - \frac{x}{a} - \frac{y}{b}\right)\)

For \(z \ge 0\), we need \(1 - \frac{x}{a} - \frac{y}{b} \ge 0\), i.e., \(\frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} \le 1\)

\[V = \int_0^a \int_0^{b(1-x/a)} \int_0^{c(1-x/a-y/b)} dz \, dy \, dx\]
Step 2: Integrate with respect to z
\[V = \int_0^a \int_0^{b(1-x/a)} c\left(1 - \frac{x}{a} - \frac{y}{b}\right) dy \, dx\]
Step 3: Integrate with respect to y

Let \(u = 1 - \frac{x}{a}\). Then \(y\) goes from 0 to \(bu\).

\[= c\int_0^a \left[\left(1-\frac{x}{a}\right)y - \frac{y^2}{2b}\right]_0^{b(1-x/a)} dx\] \[= c\int_0^a \left[ub \cdot u - \frac{(bu)^2}{2b}\right] dx\] \[= c\int_0^a \left[bu^2 - \frac{bu^2}{2}\right] dx\] \[= c\int_0^a \frac{bu^2}{2} dx = \frac{bc}{2}\int_0^a \left(1-\frac{x}{a}\right)^2 dx\]
Step 4: Integrate with respect to x

Let \(t = \frac{x}{a}\), so \(dx = a \, dt\):

\[= \frac{abc}{2}\int_0^1 (1-t)^2 \, dt\] \[= \frac{abc}{2}\left[-\frac{(1-t)^3}{3}\right]_0^1\] \[= \frac{abc}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3}\] \[= \frac{abc}{6}\]
Answer
\[V = \frac{abc}{6}\]

(This is the well-known formula for the volume of a tetrahedron with perpendicular edges of lengths \(a\), \(b\), \(c\).)

Question 16.126

Professor Leonard: Triple Integrals (Cylindrical/Spherical)

Find the volume determined by \(z \le 6 - x^2 - y^2\) and \(z \ge \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\).

Solution
The Surfaces
  • \(z = 6 - x^2 - y^2\) is a paraboloid opening downward with vertex at \((0,0,6)\)
  • \(z = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\) is a cone opening upward with vertex at the origin
Step 1: Find the intersection (in cylindrical coordinates)

Using \(r^2 = x^2 + y^2\):

  • Paraboloid: \(z = 6 - r^2\)
  • Cone: \(z = r\)

Setting them equal:

\[r = 6 - r^2\] \[r^2 + r - 6 = 0\] \[(r + 3)(r - 2) = 0\]

So \(r = 2\) (taking positive value).

Step 2: Set up the integral
\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^2 \int_r^{6-r^2} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta\]
Step 3: Integrate with respect to z
\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^2 r\left(6 - r^2 - r\right) dr \, d\theta\] \[= 2\pi \int_0^2 \left(6r - r^3 - r^2\right) dr\]
Step 4: Integrate with respect to r
\[= 2\pi \left[3r^2 - \frac{r^4}{4} - \frac{r^3}{3}\right]_0^2\] \[= 2\pi \left[3(4) - \frac{16}{4} - \frac{8}{3}\right]\] \[= 2\pi \left[12 - 4 - \frac{8}{3}\right]\] \[= 2\pi \left[8 - \frac{8}{3}\right]\] \[= 2\pi \cdot \frac{16}{3}\] \[= \frac{32\pi}{3}\]
Answer
\[V = \frac{32\pi}{3}\]
Question 16.127
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
MathisPower4U: Tetrahedron Volume Professor Leonard: Triple Integrals

The tetrahedron defined by \(x \ge 0\), \(y \ge 0\), \(z \ge 0\), \(x + y + z \le 1\) is to be sliced into \(n\) segments of equal volume by planes parallel to the plane \(x + y + z = 1\). Where should the slices be made?

Solution
Key Insight

The slicing planes have the form \(x + y + z = k\) for various values of \(k\) between 0 and 1.

The region below the plane \(x + y + z = k\) (and above the coordinate planes) is a smaller, similar tetrahedron.

Step 1: Find the volume below \(x + y + z = k\)

From Question 16.125, with \(a = b = c = 1\), the full tetrahedron has volume \(\frac{1}{6}\).

The tetrahedron below \(x + y + z = k\) is similar with scale factor \(k\), so its volume is:

\[V(k) = \frac{1}{6}k^3\]
Step 2: Set up the equal volume condition

Total volume = \(\frac{1}{6}\)

Each of the \(n\) segments should have volume \(\frac{1}{6n}\)

The \(i\)-th slice (from the bottom) should contain volume \(\frac{i}{6n}\).

Step 3: Solve for the cutting planes

We need \(V(k_i) = \frac{i}{6n}\), so:

\[\frac{1}{6}k_i^3 = \frac{i}{6n}\] \[k_i^3 = \frac{i}{n}\] \[k_i = \left(\frac{i}{n}\right)^{1/3}\]
Answer

The slices should be made at the planes:

\[x + y + z = \left(\frac{i}{n}\right)^{1/3} \quad \text{for } i = 1, 2, 3, \ldots, n-1\]

Equivalently, at \(x + y + z = \sqrt[3]{\frac{1}{n}}, \sqrt[3]{\frac{2}{n}}, \sqrt[3]{\frac{3}{n}}, \ldots, \sqrt[3]{\frac{n-1}{n}}\)

Question 16.128
ðŸ“º Helpful Videos
Professor Leonard: Triple Integrals (Spherical)

Let \(E\) be the solid ellipsoid \(E = \{(x,y,z) \mid \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} + \frac{z^2}{c^2} \le 1\}\), where \(a > 0\), \(b > 0\), and \(c > 0\). Evaluate

\[\iiint_E xyz \, dx \, dy \, dz\]

(a) over the whole ellipsoid; and

(b) over that part of it in the first quadrant: \(x \ge 0\), \(y \ge 0\), and \(z \ge 0\), \(\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} + \frac{z^2}{c^2} \le 1\).

Solution
Part (a): Over the whole ellipsoid
Symmetry Argument

The integrand \(f(x,y,z) = xyz\) is an odd function in each variable:

  • \(f(-x,y,z) = -xyz = -f(x,y,z)\)
  • \(f(x,-y,z) = -xyz = -f(x,y,z)\)
  • \(f(x,y,-z) = -xyz = -f(x,y,z)\)

The ellipsoid is symmetric about each coordinate plane (i.e., if \((x,y,z)\) is in \(E\), so is \((-x,y,z)\), etc.)

Answer (a)
\[\iiint_E xyz \, dx \, dy \, dz = 0\]

By symmetry, the contributions from opposite octants cancel.

Part (b): Over the first octant portion
Step 1: Use ellipsoidal coordinates

Substitute: \(x = a\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\), \(y = b\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\), \(z = c\rho\cos\phi\)

For the first octant: \(0 \le \rho \le 1\), \(0 \le \phi \le \frac{\pi}{2}\), \(0 \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}\)

\[= abc\rho^3\sin^2\phi\cos\phi\sin\theta\cos\theta\]

Find the volume of the "ice cream cone" defined by the inequalities \(x^2 + y^2 \le \frac{1}{5}z^2\), and \(0 \le z \le 5 + \sqrt{5 - x^2 - y^2}\).

Solution
Understanding the Shape
  • The cone \(x^2 + y^2 \le \frac{1}{5}z^2\) opens upward with vertex at origin. This is equivalent to \(r \le \frac{z}{\sqrt{5}}\) or \(z \ge \sqrt{5}r\).
  • The upper bound \(z = 5 + \sqrt{5 - x^2 - y^2} = 5 + \sqrt{5 - r^2}\) is part of a sphere centered at \((0, 0, 5)\) with radius \(\sqrt{5}\).
  • The constraint \(5 - r^2 \ge 0\) means \(r \le \sqrt{5}\).
Step 1: Find where the cone meets the spherical cap

Setting \(z = \sqrt{5}r\) equal to \(z = 5 + \sqrt{5 - r^2}\):

\[\sqrt{5}r = 5 + \sqrt{5 - r^2}\] \[\sqrt{5}r - 5 = \sqrt{5 - r^2}\]

Squaring (noting we need \(\sqrt{5}r \ge 5\), i.e., \(r \ge \sqrt{5}\)):

\[5r^2 - 10\sqrt{5}r + 25 = 5 - r^2\] \[6r^2 - 10\sqrt{5}r + 20 = 0\] \[3r^2 - 5\sqrt{5}r + 10 = 0\]

Using the quadratic formula:

\[r = \frac{5\sqrt{5} \pm \sqrt{125 - 120}}{6} = \frac{5\sqrt{5} \pm \sqrt{5}}{6}\]

So \(r = \frac{6\sqrt{5}}{6} = \sqrt{5}\) or \(r = \frac{4\sqrt{5}}{6} = \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{3}\)

Since we need \(r \ge \sqrt{5}\), the intersection occurs at \(r = \sqrt{5}\), where \(z = 5\).

Step 2: Set up the integral

The region is bounded below by the cone \(z = \sqrt{5}r\) and above by the spherical cap \(z = 5 + \sqrt{5 - r^2}\), for \(0 \le r \le \sqrt{5}\).

\[V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\sqrt{5}} \int_{\sqrt{5}r}^{5+\sqrt{5-r^2}} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta\]
Step 3: Integrate with respect to z
\[V = 2\pi \int_0^{\sqrt{5}} r\left(5 + \sqrt{5-r^2} - \sqrt{5}r\right) dr\] \[= 2\pi \int_0^{\sqrt{5}} \left(5r + r\sqrt{5-r^2} - \sqrt{5}r^2\right) dr\]
Step 4: Evaluate each part

First integral:

\[\int_0^{\sqrt{5}} 5r \, dr = 5 \cdot \frac{r^2}{2}\Big|_0^{\sqrt{5}} = \frac{5 \cdot 5}{2} = \frac{25}{2}\]

Second integral: Let \(u = 5 - r^2\), \(du = -2r \, dr\)

\[\int_0^{\sqrt{5}} r\sqrt{5-r^2} \, dr = -\frac{1}{2}\int_5^0 \sqrt{u} \, du = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^5 \sqrt{u} \, du\] \[= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\Big|_0^5 = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 5\sqrt{5} = \frac{5\sqrt{5}}{3}\]

Third integral:

\[\int_0^{\sqrt{5}} \sqrt{5}r^2 \, dr = \sqrt{5} \cdot \frac{r^3}{3}\Big|_0^{\sqrt{5}} = \sqrt{5} \cdot \frac{5\sqrt{5}}{3} = \frac{25}{3}\]
Step 5: Combine
\[V = 2\pi\left(\frac{25}{2} + \frac{5\sqrt{5}}{3} - \frac{25}{3}\right)\] \[= 2\pi\left(\frac{75}{6} - \frac{50}{6} + \frac{10\sqrt{5}}{6}\right)\] \[= 2\pi\left(\frac{25 + 10\sqrt{5}}{6}\right)\] \[= \frac{\pi(25 + 10\sqrt{5})}{3}\]
Answer
\[V = \frac{\pi(25 + 10\sqrt{5})}{3} = \frac{5\pi(5 + 2\sqrt{5})}{3}\]
