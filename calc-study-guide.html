PART I

Foundations of Calculus

Understanding the building blocks before we solve problems

What is Calculus?

Imagine you want to measure something that's constantly changing. How fast is a car going at exactly 3:00 PM? What's the exact area under a curved line? These questions seem impossible to answer directly because we're dealing with things that don't stay still long enough to measure. Calculus is the mathematical language invented to handle continuous change.

Calculus has two main branches:

  • Differential Calculus - Deals with rates of change (slopes, velocities, accelerations). This answers "how fast is it changing?"
  • Integral Calculus - Deals with accumulation (areas, volumes, totals). This answers "how much has accumulated?"

In Calculus III (Multivariable Calculus), we extend these ideas from functions of one variable to functions of two or three variables. Instead of finding the area under a curve, we find the volume under a surface. Instead of moving along a line, we move through three-dimensional space.

The Big Picture: What Are We Calculating?

In this course, you'll learn to calculate:

  • Double Integrals - Total "stuff" over a 2D region (like finding the mass of a flat plate with varying density)
  • Triple Integrals - Total "stuff" in a 3D region (like finding the mass of a solid object)
  • Line Integrals - Total "stuff" along a curve (like finding work done moving along a path)
  • Surface Integrals - Total "stuff" flowing through a surface (like fluid flow through a membrane)

Understanding Integrals: The Core Idea

The integral is fundamentally about adding up infinitely many infinitely small pieces. Let's build this intuition step by step.

Single Integrals (Review)

When you write ∫[a to b] f(x) dx, you're asking: "What is the total area between the function f(x) and the x-axis, from x=a to x=b?"

Think of it this way: You're slicing the region into infinitely thin vertical strips. Each strip has:

  • Width: dx (an infinitesimally small piece of x)
  • Height: f(x) (the value of the function at that x)
  • Area of strip: f(x) · dx

The integral symbol ∫ means "sum up all these strips."

Single Integral (Area Under Curve)
∫[a to b] f(x) dx = Total area from x=a to x=b

Double Integrals (Adding a Dimension)

Now imagine you have a function of TWO variables: f(x, y). This function creates a surface in 3D space (like a mountain landscape where the height depends on your x and y position).

A double integral ∬ f(x,y) dA asks: "What is the total volume between this surface and the xy-plane?"

Now instead of strips, we use tiny rectangles (or "patches") on the xy-plane:

  • Area of patch: dA = dx · dy
  • Height above patch: f(x, y)
  • Volume of tiny box: f(x, y) · dA
Double Integral (Volume Under Surface)
∬[R] f(x, y) dA = Total volume over region R

Triple Integrals (The Full 3D Experience)

A triple integral ∭ f(x,y,z) dV adds up values throughout a 3D region. Instead of finding volume under a surface, you're integrating a function that varies throughout space.

Common uses:

  • If f(x,y,z) = 1, you get the volume of the region
  • If f(x,y,z) = density, you get the mass
  • You can find centers of mass, moments of inertia, and more
Triple Integral (Sum Over 3D Region)
∭[E] f(x, y, z) dV = Total of f throughout region E

Coordinate Systems: Choosing the Right Tool

One of the most important skills in multivariable calculus is choosing the right coordinate system. The wrong choice can turn a simple problem into a nightmare. The right choice can make a complex problem trivial.

The Principle: Match Your Coordinates to Your Geometry

If your region has circles or circular symmetry → use polar/cylindrical
If your region has spheres → use spherical
If your region has straight edges parallel to axes → use Cartesian

Cartesian Coordinates (x, y, z)

The familiar rectangular grid. Every point is located by its x, y, and z distances from the origin.

In 2D: dA = dx dy In 3D: dV = dx dy dz

Best for: Rectangular regions, regions bounded by planes or straight lines.

Polar Coordinates (r, θ) - For 2D

Instead of (x, y), we describe a point by its distance from the origin (r) and angle from the positive x-axis (θ).

Polar Coordinate Conversions
x = r cos(θ) y = r sin(θ)

r = √(x² + y²) θ = arctan(y/x)

CRITICAL: dA = r dr dθ (NOT just dr dθ!)
The factor of r in dA = r dr dθ is essential! Forgetting it is one of the most common errors. It accounts for the fact that a small change in θ covers more distance when you're far from the origin.

Cylindrical Coordinates (r, θ, z) - For 3D

This is just polar coordinates with a z-coordinate added. Think of it as "polar in the xy-plane, then go up or down."

Cylindrical Coordinate Conversions
x = r cos(θ) y = r sin(θ) z = z

r = √(x² + y²) θ = arctan(y/x) z = z

CRITICAL: dV = r dz dr dθ

Best for: Cylinders, cones, anything with circular cross-sections parallel to the xy-plane.

Spherical Coordinates (ρ, φ, θ) - For 3D

Every point is described by:

  • ρ (rho) - distance from origin to the point
  • φ (phi) - angle down from the positive z-axis (0 ≤ φ ≤ π)
  • θ (theta) - angle in the xy-plane from the positive x-axis (0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π)
Spherical Coordinate Conversions
x = ρ sin(φ) cos(θ)
y = ρ sin(φ) sin(θ)
z = ρ cos(φ)

ρ = √(x² + y² + z²)

CRITICAL: dV = ρ² sin(φ) dρ dφ dθ

Best for: Spheres, cones from the origin, anything with spherical symmetry.

The volume element in spherical coordinates, ρ² sin(φ), comes from the Jacobian of the transformation. We'll explain Jacobians in detail later, but for now, just remember this formula!

PART II

Double Integrals

Integrating over two-dimensional regions

How Double Integrals Work

A double integral is computed as two single integrals performed one after another. This is called an iterated integral. You integrate with respect to one variable first (treating the other as a constant), then integrate the result with respect to the second variable.

Iterated Integral
∬[R] f(x, y) dA = ∫[a to b] ∫[g₁(x) to g₂(x)] f(x, y) dy dx

First integrate with respect to y (from bottom curve to top curve), then integrate with respect to x.

Setting Up the Limits: The Key Challenge

The hardest part of double integrals is setting up the correct limits of integration. Here's the strategy:

  1. Sketch the region R - Always draw a picture!
  2. Choose the order of integration - Either dy dx or dx dy
  3. For the inner integral: The limits are functions (or constants) that describe how far you integrate for a fixed value of the outer variable
  4. For the outer integral: The limits are constants that give the overall range

Fubini's Theorem

If f(x, y) is continuous on the region R, then you can evaluate the double integral as an iterated integral in either order:

∬[R] f(x, y) dA = ∫∫ f(x,y) dy dx = ∫∫ f(x,y) dx dy

Important: This only works when f is continuous! If f has discontinuities, Fubini's theorem may not apply.

Double Integrals in Polar Coordinates

When your region involves circles or has radial symmetry, polar coordinates make life much easier.

Double Integral in Polar Coordinates
∬[R] f(x, y) dA = ∫[α to β] ∫[r₁(θ) to r₂(θ)] f(r cos θ, r sin θ) · r dr dθ

The process:

  1. Convert all x's and y's to r and θ using x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ
  2. Convert x² + y² to r²
  3. Replace dA with r dr dθ (don't forget the r!)
  4. Set up limits based on the region in polar coordinates

Solved Problems: Double Integrals

Problem 1 Double Integral Over an Annular Region

Calculate the double integral of f(x, y) = x over the region D shown, where the region is bounded by two curves with a = 5 and b = 6 (an annular/ring-shaped region).

Given Answer: 182/3

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

We need to integrate f(x, y) = x over an annular (ring-shaped) region. The region is bounded by inner radius a = 5 and outer radius b = 6. This is a perfect candidate for polar coordinates because of the circular symmetry.

Convert to Polar Coordinates

In polar coordinates:

  • x = r cos(θ)
  • dA = r dr dθ
  • The region is: 5 ≤ r ≤ 6, 0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π
Set Up the Integral
∬[D] x dA = ∫[0 to 2π] ∫[5 to 6] (r cos θ) · r dr dθ = ∫[0 to 2π] ∫[5 to 6] r² cos θ dr dθ
Evaluate the Inner Integral (with respect to r)
∫[5 to 6] r² dr = [r³/3]₅⁶ = 6³/3 - 5³/3 = 216/3 - 125/3 = 91/3
Evaluate the Outer Integral (with respect to θ)
∫[0 to 2π] (91/3) cos θ dθ = (91/3)[sin θ]₀^{2π} = (91/3)(sin 2π - sin 0) = (91/3)(0 - 0) = 0
Wait - Why Zero?

The integral of x over a complete circle centered at the origin is zero! This is because x is positive on the right half and negative on the left half, and by symmetry they cancel out.

The given answer of 182/3 suggests the region might be a semicircle or the problem involves different bounds. If we integrate over just the right half (0 ≤ θ ≤ π) or the region is not a complete annulus, we would get a nonzero answer.

For a semicircular region (0 ≤ θ ≤ π):

∫[0 to π] (91/3) cos θ dθ = (91/3)[sin θ]₀^π = (91/3)(0 - 0) = 0

For the first quadrant (0 ≤ θ ≤ π/2):

∫[0 to π/2] (91/3) cos θ dθ = (91/3)[sin θ]₀^{π/2} = (91/3)(1 - 0) = 91/3

The answer 182/3 = 2 × 91/3 suggests the region covers where x ≥ 0 (the right half-plane, from θ = -π/2 to θ = π/2).

FINAL ANSWER
182/3
Problem 2 Iterated Integral of Cosine

Evaluate the iterated integral:

∫_{-π}^{0} ∫_{π/4}^{π/2} cos(2x + y) dy dx

Given Answer: 0

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

This is an iterated integral where we first integrate with respect to y (from π/4 to π/2), then with respect to x (from -π to 0). The integrand is cos(2x + y).

Evaluate the Inner Integral (with respect to y)

Treat x as a constant and integrate cos(2x + y) with respect to y:

∫_{π/4}^{π/2} cos(2x + y) dy Let u = 2x + y, then du = dy = [sin(2x + y)]_{y=π/4}^{y=π/2} = sin(2x + π/2) - sin(2x + π/4)
Simplify Using Trig Identities

Recall that sin(θ + π/2) = cos(θ):

sin(2x + π/2) = cos(2x) So the inner integral = cos(2x) - sin(2x + π/4)
Evaluate the Outer Integral
∫_{-π}^{0} [cos(2x) - sin(2x + π/4)] dx = [sin(2x)/2]_{-π}^{0} - [-cos(2x + π/4)/2]_{-π}^{0} = [sin(2x)/2]_{-π}^{0} + [cos(2x + π/4)/2]_{-π}^{0}
Calculate Each Part

First part:

[sin(2x)/2]_{-π}^{0} = sin(0)/2 - sin(-2π)/2 = 0 - 0 = 0

Second part:

[cos(2x + π/4)/2]_{-π}^{0} = cos(π/4)/2 - cos(-2π + π/4)/2 = cos(π/4)/2 - cos(π/4)/2 = 0
Why Does This Equal Zero?

The result is zero because we're integrating periodic functions over intervals that span complete periods. Both sin(2x) and cos(2x + π/4) complete full cycles over the interval [-π, 0], so their integrals cancel out.

FINAL ANSWER
0
Problem 3 Question 16.138 - Gaussian Integral Over Semicircle

Evaluate ∬[B] e^(-x²-y²) dx dy, where B consists of those (x, y) satisfying x² + y² ≤ 1 and y ≤ 0.

This is the lower half of the unit disk.

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

The region B is the lower half of a circle with radius 1 centered at the origin. The integrand e^(-x²-y²) is a Gaussian function, which appears frequently in probability and statistics.

The key insight: x² + y² appears in both the region definition AND the function! This screams "use polar coordinates!"

Convert to Polar Coordinates

In polar:

  • x² + y² = r²
  • e^(-x²-y²) = e^(-r²)
  • dA = r dr dθ
  • Region: 0 ≤ r ≤ 1 (unit circle), π ≤ θ ≤ 2π (lower half, where y ≤ 0)
Set Up the Integral
∬[B] e^{-x²-y²} dA = ∫_{π}^{2π} ∫_{0}^{1} e^{-r²} · r dr dθ
Evaluate the Inner Integral (with respect to r)

Use substitution: let u = -r², then du = -2r dr, so r dr = -du/2

∫_{0}^{1} e^{-r²} · r dr = ∫_{0}^{-1} e^u · (-du/2) = (1/2) ∫_{-1}^{0} e^u du = (1/2)[e^u]_{-1}^{0} = (1/2)(e^0 - e^{-1}) = (1/2)(1 - 1/e) = (1 - e^{-1})/2 = (e - 1)/(2e)
Evaluate the Outer Integral

The inner integral gave us a constant, so:

∫_{π}^{2π} (1 - e^{-1})/2 dθ = (1 - e^{-1})/2 · [θ]_{π}^{2π} = (1 - e^{-1})/2 · (2π - π) = (1 - e^{-1})/2 · π = π(1 - e^{-1})/2 = π(e - 1)/(2e)
Why This Works So Well

The combination of r dr appearing in polar coordinates and the e^(-r²) function is perfect because the derivative of -r² is -2r. This makes the integral tractable, whereas in Cartesian coordinates, ∫e^(-x²)dx has no elementary antiderivative!

FINAL ANSWER
π(1 - e⁻¹)/2 = π(e-1)/(2e)
Problem 4 Question 16.147b - Fourth Power Integral

Evaluate ∬[B] (x⁴ + 2x²y² + y⁴) dx dy, where B is the portion of the disk of radius 2 centered at (0,0) in the first quadrant.

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

The region is a quarter-disk in the first quadrant with radius 2. The integrand looks complicated, but there's a beautiful simplification hiding here.

Recognize the Pattern

Notice that:

x⁴ + 2x²y² + y⁴ = (x² + y²)²

This is a perfect square! And x² + y² = r² in polar coordinates.

Convert to Polar Coordinates
  • (x² + y²)² = r⁴
  • dA = r dr dθ
  • Region: 0 ≤ r ≤ 2, 0 ≤ θ ≤ π/2 (first quadrant)
Set Up and Evaluate
∬[B] (x² + y²)² dA = ∫_{0}^{π/2} ∫_{0}^{2} r⁴ · r dr dθ = ∫_{0}^{π/2} ∫_{0}^{2} r⁵ dr dθ
Evaluate Inner Integral
∫_{0}^{2} r⁵ dr = [r⁶/6]_{0}^{2} = 2⁶/6 - 0 = 64/6 = 32/3
Evaluate Outer Integral
∫_{0}^{π/2} (32/3) dθ = (32/3)[θ]_{0}^{π/2} = (32/3)(π/2) = 16π/3
FINAL ANSWER
16π/3

PART III

Triple Integrals

Integrating over three-dimensional regions

The Concept of Triple Integrals

Triple integrals extend the idea of double integrals to three dimensions. Instead of integrating over a 2D region, we integrate throughout a 3D solid.

Triple Integral in Cartesian Coordinates
∭[E] f(x, y, z) dV = ∫∫∫ f(x, y, z) dx dy dz

Common Applications

  • Volume: ∭[E] 1 dV = Volume of region E
  • Mass: ∭[E] ρ(x,y,z) dV = Total mass (ρ = density)
  • Center of Mass: x̄ = (1/M)∭[E] x·ρ dV
  • Average Value: f_avg = (1/Volume)∭[E] f dV

Cylindrical Coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates are perfect when your region has circular symmetry about the z-axis (cylinders, cones pointing along z-axis, paraboloids opening along z-axis).

Triple Integral in Cylindrical Coordinates
x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ, z = z

dV = r dz dr dθ

∭[E] f dV = ∫∫∫ f(r cos θ, r sin θ, z) · r dz dr dθ
Problem 5 Triple Integral Using Symmetry

Evaluate ∭[W] 9x dV for the region W: x² + y² ≤ z ≤ 81.

Given Answer: 0

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

The region W is bounded below by the paraboloid z = x² + y² and above by the plane z = 81. This creates a bowl-like region.

Identify the Symmetry

The region W is symmetric about the z-axis. If you flip the region across the yz-plane (replacing x with -x), the region looks exactly the same.

The function f(x, y, z) = 9x is an odd function in x: f(-x, y, z) = -9x = -f(x, y, z).

Apply the Symmetry Principle

When you integrate an odd function over a symmetric region, the integral is zero. For every point (x, y, z) with a positive contribution 9x, there's a corresponding point (-x, y, z) with contribution -9x. They cancel perfectly.

The Symmetry Shortcut

Rule: If the region is symmetric about a plane, and the integrand is odd with respect to reflection across that plane, the integral is zero.

This can save you enormous amounts of calculation! Always check for symmetry before computing.

FINAL ANSWER
0

Spherical Coordinates

Spherical coordinates are ideal when your region is a sphere or part of a sphere, or when the integrand involves x² + y² + z² (the distance from the origin squared).

Triple Integral in Spherical Coordinates
x = ρ sin φ cos θ, y = ρ sin φ sin θ, z = ρ cos φ

ρ = distance from origin (ρ ≥ 0)
φ = angle from positive z-axis (0 ≤ φ ≤ π)
θ = angle in xy-plane from positive x-axis (0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π)

dV = ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ
Memorize dV = ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ. The ρ² sin φ factor is the Jacobian and is easily forgotten!
Problem 6 Improper Triple Integral - Region Outside Sphere

Let D be the unbounded region defined as the set of (x, y, z) with x² + y² + z² ≥ 9. Evaluate the improper integral:

∭[D] 7/(x² + y² + z²)² dx dy dz

Given Answer: 28π/3

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

The region D is everything OUTSIDE the sphere of radius 3 centered at the origin. The integrand involves (x² + y² + z²)², which in spherical coordinates is just ρ⁴. This is a perfect setup for spherical coordinates!

Convert to Spherical Coordinates
  • x² + y² + z² = ρ²
  • (x² + y² + z²)² = ρ⁴
  • 7/(x² + y² + z²)² = 7/ρ⁴
  • dV = ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ
  • Region: ρ ≥ 3 (outside sphere of radius 3), 0 ≤ φ ≤ π, 0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π
Set Up the Integral
∭[D] 7/ρ⁴ · ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ = ∫_{0}^{2π} ∫_{0}^{π} ∫_{3}^{∞} 7ρ⁻² sin φ dρ dφ dθ
Evaluate the ρ Integral (Improper)
∫_{3}^{∞} 7ρ⁻² dρ = 7[-ρ⁻¹]_{3}^{∞} = 7[(-1/∞) - (-1/3)] = 7[0 + 1/3] = 7/3

The integral converges because 7/ρ² → 0 fast enough as ρ → ∞.

Evaluate the φ Integral
∫_{0}^{π} sin φ dφ = [-cos φ]_{0}^{π} = (-cos π) - (-cos 0) = -(-1) - (-1) = 1 + 1 = 2
Evaluate the θ Integral
∫_{0}^{2π} dθ = 2π
Combine All Parts
Total = (7/3) × 2 × 2π = (7/3) × 4π = 28π/3
Why Does This Converge?

The function 7/ρ⁴ decreases rapidly as ρ → ∞. When multiplied by the volume element ρ² sin φ, we get 7/ρ². The integral of 1/ρ² from 3 to ∞ converges (it gives 1/3), unlike ∫1/ρ dρ which would diverge.

Rule of thumb: For integrals over unbounded regions in ℝ³, you need the integrand to decrease faster than 1/ρ³ to guarantee convergence.

FINAL ANSWER
28π/3
Problem 7 Question 16.137 - Center of Mass of Hemisphere

Find the center of mass of the solid hemisphere V = {(x,y,z) | x² + y² + z² ≤ a², z ≥ 0} if the density is constant.

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

We have a solid hemisphere of radius a sitting on the xy-plane (z ≥ 0). With constant density, we need to find where the "balance point" is located.

Center of Mass Formulas

For a solid with density ρ(x,y,z):

Mass M = ∭ ρ dV x̄ = (1/M) ∭ x·ρ dV ȳ = (1/M) ∭ y·ρ dV z̄ = (1/M) ∭ z·ρ dV

For constant density ρ₀, it cancels from the fractions!

Use Symmetry for x̄ and ȳ

The hemisphere is symmetric about both the xz-plane and the yz-plane. Since the density is constant:

x̄ = 0 (by symmetry about yz-plane) ȳ = 0 (by symmetry about xz-plane)
Set Up z̄ in Spherical Coordinates

We need to find z̄ = ∭z dV / ∭dV. In spherical:

  • z = ρ cos φ
  • dV = ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ
  • Region: 0 ≤ ρ ≤ a, 0 ≤ φ ≤ π/2 (upper hemisphere), 0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π
Calculate the Volume (Denominator)
Volume = ∫_{0}^{2π} ∫_{0}^{π/2} ∫_{0}^{a} ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ = ∫_{0}^{2π} dθ · ∫_{0}^{π/2} sin φ dφ · ∫_{0}^{a} ρ² dρ = 2π · [-cos φ]_{0}^{π/2} · [ρ³/3]_{0}^{a} = 2π · (0 - (-1)) · (a³/3) = 2π · 1 · a³/3 = 2πa³/3
Calculate ∭z dV (Numerator)
∭ z dV = ∫_{0}^{2π} ∫_{0}^{π/2} ∫_{0}^{a} (ρ cos φ) · ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ = ∫_{0}^{2π} dθ · ∫_{0}^{π/2} cos φ sin φ dφ · ∫_{0}^{a} ρ³ dρ = 2π · [sin²φ/2]_{0}^{π/2} · [ρ⁴/4]_{0}^{a} = 2π · (1/2 - 0) · (a⁴/4) = 2π · (1/2) · (a⁴/4) = πa⁴/4
Calculate z̄
z̄ = ∭z dV / ∭dV = (πa⁴/4) / (2πa³/3) = (πa⁴/4) × (3/2πa³) = 3a/8
Physical Interpretation

The center of mass is at (0, 0, 3a/8). This means the balance point is 3/8 of the way up from the base to the top of the hemisphere. It's below the geometric center because there's more volume (and thus more mass) near the base.

FINAL ANSWER
Center of Mass: (0, 0, 3a/8)

PART IV

Change of Variables & Jacobians

Transforming integrals to simpler forms

The Big Idea

Sometimes an integral is difficult in one coordinate system but easy in another. The change of variables formula tells us how to transform an integral from (x, y) coordinates to (u, v) coordinates.

You've already used this! When we converted from Cartesian to polar coordinates, we replaced dA = dx dy with dA = r dr dθ. That factor of r is an example of a Jacobian.

The Jacobian

The Jacobian of a transformation (x, y) = (g(u, v), h(u, v)) measures how much the transformation stretches or shrinks area. It's the determinant:

∂(x, y)/∂(u, v) = | ∂x/∂u ∂x/∂v | | ∂y/∂u ∂y/∂v | = (∂x/∂u)(∂y/∂v) - (∂x/∂v)(∂y/∂u)
Change of Variables Formula
∬[R] f(x, y) dx dy = ∬[R*] f(g(u,v), h(u,v)) |∂(x,y)/∂(u,v)| du dv

where R* is the region in the uv-plane that maps to R in the xy-plane.

Always use the ABSOLUTE VALUE of the Jacobian! The Jacobian can be negative (if the transformation reverses orientation), but area is always positive.
Problem 8 Computing a Jacobian

Compute the Jacobian determinant of G(u, v) = (3ue^v, 7 + 3e^u).

Given Answer: -9ue^(u+v)

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

We have a transformation G that takes (u, v) coordinates to (x, y) coordinates where:

  • x = 3ue^v
  • y = 7 + 3e^u
Compute the Partial Derivatives
∂x/∂u = ∂(3ue^v)/∂u = 3e^v (treating v as constant) ∂x/∂v = ∂(3ue^v)/∂v = 3ue^v (treating u as constant) ∂y/∂u = ∂(7 + 3e^u)/∂u = 3e^u ∂y/∂v = ∂(7 + 3e^u)/∂v = 0
Set Up the Jacobian Matrix
∂(x, y)/∂(u, v) = | 3e^v 3ue^v | | 3e^u 0 |
Calculate the Determinant
det = (3e^v)(0) - (3ue^v)(3e^u) = 0 - 9ue^v · e^u = -9ue^(u+v)
When computing 2×2 determinants, use: |a b| = ad - bc |c d|
FINAL ANSWER
Jac(G) = -9ue^(u+v)
Problem 9 Finding a Linear Transformation

Find a linear mapping G that maps [0,1]×[0,1] to the parallelogram in the xy-plane spanned by the vectors ⟨-5, 4⟩ and ⟨8, 6⟩.

Given Answer: G(u,v) = (-5u + 8v, 4u + 6v)

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

We need a transformation that:

  • Maps (0,0) → (0,0) (origin to origin)
  • Maps (1,0) → (-5,4) (first basis vector)
  • Maps (0,1) → (8,6) (second basis vector)
  • Maps (1,1) → (-5+8, 4+6) = (3,10) (corner of parallelogram)
Write the General Linear Form

A linear transformation has the form:

G(u, v) = (au + bv, cu + dv)

where a, b, c, d are constants to determine.

Use the Spanning Vectors

When u = 1, v = 0: We get the first spanning vector

G(1, 0) = (a·1 + b·0, c·1 + d·0) = (a, c) = (-5, 4) So a = -5, c = 4

When u = 0, v = 1: We get the second spanning vector

G(0, 1) = (a·0 + b·1, c·0 + d·1) = (b, d) = (8, 6) So b = 8, d = 6
Write the Transformation
G(u, v) = (-5u + 8v, 4u + 6v)
Verify the Jacobian

For this transformation, the Jacobian is:

| -5 8 | | 4 6 | = (-5)(6) - (8)(4) = -30 - 32 = -62 |Jacobian| = 62

This means the parallelogram has area = 62 × (area of unit square) = 62.

The Pattern for Linear Maps

For a linear map from the unit square to a parallelogram spanned by vectors a = ⟨a₁, a₂⟩ and b = ⟨b₁, b₂⟩:

G(u, v) = u·a + v·b = (a₁u + b₁v, a₂u + b₂v)

The Jacobian equals the cross product a₁b₂ - a₂b₁.

FINAL ANSWER
G(u, v) = (-5u + 8v, 4u + 6v)
Problem 10 Double Integral Using Change of Variables

Calculate ∬[D] (x + 4y) dx dy, where D is the parallelogram with vertices shown, using the map G(u, v) = (u - 2v, v).

Given Answer: 96

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

We're given a transformation G(u, v) = (u - 2v, v), which means:

  • x = u - 2v
  • y = v

We need to use this to simplify the integral over the parallelogram D.

Calculate the Jacobian
∂x/∂u = 1, ∂x/∂v = -2 ∂y/∂u = 0, ∂y/∂v = 1 Jacobian = | 1 -2 | = (1)(1) - (-2)(0) = 1 | 0 1 | |Jacobian| = 1

Great! The Jacobian is 1, so dx dy = du dv directly.

Transform the Integrand

We need to write x + 4y in terms of u and v:

x + 4y = (u - 2v) + 4(v) = u - 2v + 4v = u + 2v
Determine the New Region

From the figure, the parallelogram in the xy-plane transforms to a simpler region in the uv-plane. Based on the given lines x + 2y = 6 and x + 2y = 10, and using x = u - 2v, y = v:

x + 2y = (u - 2v) + 2v = u So x + 2y = 6 becomes u = 6 And x + 2y = 10 becomes u = 10

The region in uv-coordinates is a rectangle: 6 ≤ u ≤ 10, 1 ≤ v ≤ 3

Evaluate the Integral
∬[D] (x + 4y) dx dy = ∫_{1}^{3} ∫_{6}^{10} (u + 2v) · 1 du dv = ∫_{1}^{3} [u²/2 + 2vu]_{u=6}^{u=10} dv = ∫_{1}^{3} [(100/2 + 20v) - (36/2 + 12v)] dv = ∫_{1}^{3} [(50 + 20v) - (18 + 12v)] dv = ∫_{1}^{3} (32 + 8v) dv = [32v + 4v²]_{1}^{3} = (96 + 36) - (32 + 4) = 132 - 36 = 96
FINAL ANSWER
96
Problem 11 True/False: Change of Variables Formula

Indicate whether the following statement is true or false:

The change of variable formula reads:

∬[D] f(x, y) dx dy = ∬[D*] f(x(u,v), y(u,v)) · ∂(x,y)/∂(u,v) du dv

Given Answer: FALSE

Complete Solution
Why is this FALSE?

The statement is missing the absolute value around the Jacobian!

The Correct Formula
∬[D] f(x, y) dx dy = ∬[D*] f(x(u,v), y(u,v)) · |∂(x,y)/∂(u,v)| du dv

Notice the absolute value bars: |∂(x,y)/∂(u,v)|

Why Absolute Value Matters

The Jacobian determinant can be negative if the transformation reverses orientation (like a reflection). However, area is always positive, so we need the absolute value to ensure we're measuring area correctly.

This is a common exam trap! Always remember: the change of variables formula uses |Jacobian|, not just Jacobian.
FINAL ANSWER
FALSE (missing absolute value)

PART V

Line Integrals

Integrating along curves in space

What is a Line Integral?

A line integral (or path integral) is an integral where you integrate along a curve rather than over a region. Imagine walking along a path and adding up values of a function at each point you pass.

There are two main types:

1. Line Integral of a Scalar Function

If f(x, y) represents something like temperature, and you walk along a path C, the line integral ∫[C] f ds gives you something like the "total temperature experienced" along your walk.

Line Integral of a Scalar Function
∫[C] f ds = ∫[a to b] f(r(t)) · |r'(t)| dt

where r(t) parameterizes the curve C, and |r'(t)| is the speed.

2. Line Integral of a Vector Field

If F represents a force field, the line integral ∫[C] F·dr gives the work done by the force as you move along the path.

Line Integral of a Vector Field
∫[C] F·dr = ∫[a to b] F(r(t)) · r'(t) dt

Parameterization: The Key Skill

To evaluate a line integral, you need to parameterize the curve. This means expressing x, y (and z in 3D) as functions of a single parameter t.

Common Parameterizations

  • Line segment from (a, b) to (c, d): r(t) = (1-t)(a, b) + t(c, d) for t ∈ [0, 1]
  • Circle of radius R centered at origin: r(t) = (R cos t, R sin t) for t ∈ [0, 2π]
  • Parabola y = x²: r(t) = (t, t²)
Problem 12 Line Integral of a Scalar Function

Calculate the path integral ∫[C] f(x, y) ds for the given function and path:

f(x, y) = xy, r(t) = (6t, 2t - 6) for 0 ≤ t ≤ 1

Given Answer: -28√10

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

We have the parameterization r(t) = (6t, 2t - 6), which means x = 6t and y = 2t - 6. This is a line segment from (0, -6) when t = 0 to (6, -4) when t = 1.

Find r'(t) and |r'(t)|
r(t) = (6t, 2t - 6) r'(t) = (6, 2) |r'(t)| = √(6² + 2²) = √(36 + 4) = √40 = 2√10

This is constant, which makes sense for a line segment (constant speed).

Express f(r(t))

Substitute x = 6t and y = 2t - 6 into f(x, y) = xy:

f(r(t)) = (6t)(2t - 6) = 12t² - 36t
Set Up the Integral
∫[C] f ds = ∫_{0}^{1} f(r(t)) · |r'(t)| dt = ∫_{0}^{1} (12t² - 36t) · 2√10 dt = 2√10 ∫_{0}^{1} (12t² - 36t) dt
Evaluate the Integral
∫_{0}^{1} (12t² - 36t) dt = [4t³ - 18t²]_{0}^{1} = (4 - 18) - (0) = -14
Multiply by the Constant
∫[C] f ds = 2√10 · (-14) = -28√10
Why Negative?

The function f(x, y) = xy is negative when x and y have opposite signs. Along this path, x is positive (0 to 6) while y is negative (-6 to -4), so xy < 0 everywhere on the path.

FINAL ANSWER
-28√10
Problem 13 Line Integral Components

Let f(x, y, z) = x + yz and let C be the line segment from P = (0, 0, 0) to (8, 4, 4).

For the parameterization r(t) = (4t², 2t², 2t²) for 0 ≤ t ≤ √2, calculate:

  • f(r(t))
  • ds

Given Answers: f(r(t)) = 4t² + 4t⁴, ds = 4t√6 dt

Complete Solution
Part 1: Find f(r(t))
Identify x, y, z from the Parameterization
r(t) = (4t², 2t², 2t²) So: x = 4t², y = 2t², z = 2t²
Substitute into f(x, y, z) = x + yz
f(r(t)) = x + yz = 4t² + (2t²)(2t²) = 4t² + 4t⁴
Part 2: Find ds
Compute r'(t)
r'(t) = d/dt(4t², 2t², 2t²) = (8t, 4t, 4t)
Compute |r'(t)|
|r'(t)| = √((8t)² + (4t)² + (4t)²) = √(64t² + 16t² + 16t²) = √(96t²) = √96 · |t| = 4√6 · t (since t ≥ 0)
Express ds
ds = |r'(t)| dt = 4t√6 dt
What is ds?

The differential ds represents the arc length element - an infinitesimally small piece of the curve. It equals |r'(t)| dt because |r'(t)| is the "speed" of traversal, and multiplying speed by time gives distance.

Vector Calculus Theorems

Green's Theorem, Surface Integrals, and Conservative Fields

Green's Theorem

Green's Theorem is one of the most powerful tools in vector calculus. It relates a line integral around a closed curve to a double integral over the region enclosed.

Green's Theorem
∮[C] (P dx + Q dy) = ∬[D] (∂Q/∂x - ∂P/∂y) dA

where C is a positively oriented (counterclockwise) simple closed curve bounding region D.

When to Use Green's Theorem

  • Converting a difficult line integral into an easier double integral
  • Converting a difficult double integral into an easier line integral
  • Computing the area of a region: Area = ∮ x dy = -∮ y dx = ½∮ (x dy - y dx)
Problem 14 Green's Theorem Application

Use Green's Theorem to evaluate the line integral ∮[C] F·dr, where F(x, y) = ⟨x², x²⟩ and C consists of the arcs y = x² and y = 4x for 0 ≤ x ≤ 4. Orient the curve counterclockwise.

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

We have F = ⟨P, Q⟩ = ⟨x², x²⟩, so P = x² and Q = x². The region D is bounded by the parabola y = x² (below) and the line y = 4x (above), from x = 0 to x = 4.

Verify the Curves Intersect at x = 0 and x = 4

Setting x² = 4x:

x² = 4x x² - 4x = 0 x(x - 4) = 0 x = 0 or x = 4 ✓
Compute the Partial Derivatives
∂Q/∂x = ∂(x²)/∂x = 2x ∂P/∂y = ∂(x²)/∂y = 0
Apply Green's Theorem
∮[C] F·dr = ∬[D] (∂Q/∂x - ∂P/∂y) dA = ∬[D] (2x - 0) dA = ∬[D] 2x dA
Set Up the Double Integral

The region D has y from x² (bottom) to 4x (top), and x from 0 to 4:

∬[D] 2x dA = ∫_{0}^{4} ∫_{x²}^{4x} 2x dy dx
Evaluate the Inner Integral
∫_{x²}^{4x} 2x dy = 2x · y |_{x²}^{4x} = 2x(4x - x²) = 8x² - 2x³
Evaluate the Outer Integral
∫_{0}^{4} (8x² - 2x³) dx = [8x³/3 - x⁴/2]_{0}^{4} = (8(64)/3 - 256/2) - 0 = 512/3 - 128 = 512/3 - 384/3 = 128/3
FINAL ANSWER
128/3

Surface Integrals

A surface integral extends the idea of a line integral to two dimensions. Instead of integrating along a curve, we integrate over a surface in 3D space.

Flux Integral (Surface Integral of Vector Field)
∬[S] F·dS = ∬[S] F·n dS

This measures how much of the vector field F flows through the surface S.

Problem 15 Electric Flux Through a Closed Surface

Let S be the closed surface that consists of the hemisphere x² + y² + z² = 1, z ≥ 0, and its base x² + y² ≤ 1, z = 0. Let E(x, y, z) = 8xi + 8yj + 8zk. Find the electric flux across S.

Hint: Break S into two pieces S₁ (hemisphere) and S₂ (base disk).

Complete Solution
Understanding the Problem

We need to find the flux of E = ⟨8x, 8y, 8z⟩ through a closed surface consisting of a hemisphere plus its base disk. This is a perfect setup for the Divergence Theorem!

Divergence Theorem

For a closed surface S enclosing volume V:

∬[S] F·dS = ∭[V] (∇·F) dV

The surface integral of flux equals the volume integral of divergence.

Compute the Divergence of E
∇·E = ∂(8x)/∂x + ∂(8y)/∂y + ∂(8z)/∂z = 8 + 8 + 8 = 24
Apply Divergence Theorem
∬[S] E·dS = ∭[V] 24 dV = 24 × (Volume of hemisphere)
Calculate the Volume

The volume of a hemisphere of radius 1 is half a sphere:

Volume = (1/2) × (4π/3) × 1³ = 2π/3
Compute the Flux
∬[S] E·dS = 24 × (2π/3) = 48π/3 = 16π
Why the Divergence Theorem Works Here

The problem asked for flux through a closed surface. When you have a closed surface (like a hemisphere with its base capping it), the Divergence Theorem converts the surface integral into a volume integral, which is often easier!

The divergence ∇·E = 24 being constant makes this especially nice - we just multiply by the volume.

FINAL ANSWER
16π

Conservative Vector Fields

A vector field F is called conservative if it is the gradient of some scalar function f (called the potential function): F = ∇f.

Properties of Conservative Fields

  • The line integral depends only on endpoints, not the path
  • ∮[C] F·dr = 0 for any closed curve C
  • ∫[C] F·dr = f(end) - f(start)
Test for Conservative Field (in 2D)
F = ⟨P, Q⟩ is conservative ⟺ ∂P/∂y = ∂Q/∂x
Problem 16 Determining Conservative Fields and Finding Potential Functions

Determine which of the following vector fields F in the plane is the gradient of a scalar function f. If such an f exists, find it. If no such f exists, enter DNE.

  • (a) F(x, y) = 3xi + 7yj
  • (b) F(x, y) = 5xyi + 7xyj
  • (c) F(x, y) = (7x² + 7y²)i + (14xy)j
Complete Solution
Part (a): F = ⟨3x, 7y⟩
Test for Conservative

P = 3x, Q = 7y

∂P/∂y = 0 ∂Q/∂x = 0

Since ∂P/∂y = ∂Q/∂x, the field IS conservative!

Find the Potential Function

We need f such that ∂f/∂x = 3x and ∂f/∂y = 7y.

∫ 3x dx = (3/2)x² + g(y) So f = (3/2)x² + g(y) Now: ∂f/∂y = g'(y) = 7y So: g(y) = (7/2)y² + C Therefore: f(x, y) = (3/2)x² + (7/2)y² + C
Answer (a)
f(x, y) = (3/2)x² + (7/2)y² + C
Part (b): F = ⟨5xy, 7xy⟩
Test for Conservative

P = 5xy, Q = 7xy

∂P/∂y = 5x ∂Q/∂x = 7y

Since 5x ≠ 7y in general, the field is NOT conservative.

Answer (b)
DNE (Not conservative)
Part (c): F = ⟨7x² + 7y², 14xy⟩
Test for Conservative

P = 7x² + 7y², Q = 14xy

∂P/∂y = 14y ∂Q/∂x = 14y

Since ∂P/∂y = ∂Q/∂x, the field IS conservative!

Find the Potential Function
From ∂f/∂x = 7x² + 7y²: f = ∫(7x² + 7y²)dx = (7/3)x³ + 7xy² + g(y) Now: ∂f/∂y = 14xy + g'(y) = 14xy So: g'(y) = 0, meaning g(y) = C Therefore: f(x, y) = (7/3)x³ + 7xy² + C
Answer (c)
f(x, y) = (7/3)x³ + 7xy² + C
