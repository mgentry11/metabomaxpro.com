Key Concepts Review

Change of Variables in Double Integrals

When integrating over complex regions, we can transform to simpler coordinates using a mapping $G: D_0 \to D$. The key is computing the Jacobian, which measures how area changes under the transformation.

Change of Variables Formula

$$\iint_D f(x,y)\, dA = \iint_{D_0} f(G(u,v)) \left| \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} \right| du\, dv$$

The Jacobian Determinant

For a transformation $G(u,v) = (x(u,v), y(u,v))$, the Jacobian is:

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}$$

Why Use Change of Variables?

Complex regions (parallelograms, ellipses, etc.) become simple rectangles or circles under the right transformation. This makes setting up integral bounds much easier!

Line Integrals

There are two types of line integrals:

  • Scalar Line Integrals: $\int_C f\, ds$ - integrate a function along a curve with respect to arc length
  • Vector Line Integrals: $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ - measure work done by a force field along a path

Line Integral Formulas

$$\int_C f\, ds = \int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|\, dt$$ $$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)\, dt$$

Centroid of a 3D Region

For a region $W$ with uniform density $\delta = 1$, the centroid $(\bar{x}, \bar{y}, \bar{z})$ is found by:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{V}\iiint_W x\, dV, \quad \bar{y} = \frac{1}{V}\iiint_W y\, dV, \quad \bar{z} = \frac{1}{V}\iiint_W z\, dV$$

where $V = \iiint_W dV$ is the volume of the region.

Problem 1: Change of Variables (Double Integral)

Problem Statement

Let $D$ be the parallelogram in the xy-plane spanned by the vectors $\langle 9, 8 \rangle$ and $\langle 3, 4 \rangle$.

Apply the Change of Variables Formula to the map $G(u,v) = (3u + 9v, 4u + 8v)$ to evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_D xy\, dA$ as an integral over $D_0 = [0,1] \times [0,1]$.

(Give answer as a whole number)

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Computing Jacobian determinants
  • Change of Variables formula for double integrals
  • Basic double integral evaluation
  • Understanding how linear maps transform parallelograms

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Verify the mapping transforms $D_0$ to $D$

The map $G(u,v) = (3u + 9v, 4u + 8v)$ is linear. Let's check that it maps the unit square to our parallelogram:

  • $G(0,0) = (0, 0)$ - origin maps to origin
  • $G(1,0) = (3, 4)$ - corresponds to vector $\langle 3, 4 \rangle$
  • $G(0,1) = (9, 8)$ - corresponds to vector $\langle 9, 8 \rangle$
  • $G(1,1) = (12, 12)$ - the opposite corner
2
Compute the Jacobian determinant

With $x = 3u + 9v$ and $y = 4u + 8v$:

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 3 & 9 \\ 4 & 8 \end{vmatrix}$$

Computing the determinant:

$$= (3)(8) - (9)(4) = 24 - 36 = -12$$

The absolute value is $|J| = 12$.

Why is the Jacobian Negative?

A negative Jacobian indicates the mapping reverses orientation (like a reflection). Since we only care about area scaling, we use the absolute value $|J| = 12$. This tells us that infinitesimal areas are scaled by a factor of 12.

3
Set up the transformed integral

Using the Change of Variables formula:

$$\iint_D xy\, dA = \iint_{D_0} (3u + 9v)(4u + 8v) \cdot 12\, du\, dv$$
4
Expand the integrand

First, multiply $(3u + 9v)(4u + 8v)$:

$$= 12u^2 + 24uv + 36uv + 72v^2 = 12u^2 + 60uv + 72v^2$$

Multiply by the Jacobian factor 12:

$$= 144u^2 + 720uv + 864v^2$$
5
Evaluate the integral $$\int_0^1 \int_0^1 (144u^2 + 720uv + 864v^2)\, du\, dv$$

First, integrate with respect to $u$:

$$\int_0^1 \left[ 48u^3 + 360u^2v + 864v^2u \right]_0^1 dv = \int_0^1 (48 + 360v + 864v^2)\, dv$$

Now integrate with respect to $v$:

$$= \left[ 48v + 180v^2 + 288v^3 \right]_0^1 = 48 + 180 + 288 = 516$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{516}$$

Practice Tips

  • Always verify that your transformation maps the simple region to the complex region correctly
  • Double-check your Jacobian calculation - errors here propagate through the entire problem
  • Remember: $|J|$ for area, but sign matters for orientation questions
  • For linear transformations, the Jacobian is constant, making integration easier

Problem 2: Ellipse Area via Change of Variables

Problem Statement

Compute the area of the region enclosed by the ellipse $x^2 + 2xy + 2y^2 - 4y = 45$ as an integral in the variables $u = x + y$, $v = y - 2$.

(Use symbolic notation and fractions where needed.)

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Completing the square for quadratic forms
  • Inverse transformations and Jacobians
  • Standard ellipse equation: $\frac{u^2}{a^2} + \frac{v^2}{b^2} = 1$
  • Area formula for ellipses

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Express $x$ and $y$ in terms of $u$ and $v$

Given: $u = x + y$ and $v = y - 2$

From the second equation: $y = v + 2$

Substituting into the first: $u = x + (v + 2)$, so $x = u - v - 2$

2
Substitute into the ellipse equation

We have $x = u - v - 2$ and $y = v + 2$. Substitute into $x^2 + 2xy + 2y^2 - 4y = 45$:

Calculate $x^2$:

$$x^2 = (u - v - 2)^2 = u^2 - 2uv - 4u + v^2 + 4v + 4$$

Calculate $2xy$:

$$2xy = 2(u - v - 2)(v + 2) = 2(uv + 2u - v^2 - 2v - 2v - 4)$$ $$= 2(uv + 2u - v^2 - 4v - 4) = 2uv + 4u - 2v^2 - 8v - 8$$

Calculate $2y^2$:

$$2y^2 = 2(v + 2)^2 = 2(v^2 + 4v + 4) = 2v^2 + 8v + 8$$

Calculate $-4y$:

$$-4y = -4(v + 2) = -4v - 8$$
3
Combine all terms

Adding everything:

$$x^2 + 2xy + 2y^2 - 4y = (u^2 - 2uv - 4u + v^2 + 4v + 4)$$ $$+ (2uv + 4u - 2v^2 - 8v - 8) + (2v^2 + 8v + 8) + (-4v - 8)$$

Collecting like terms:

  • $u^2$ terms: $u^2$
  • $uv$ terms: $-2uv + 2uv = 0$
  • $u$ terms: $-4u + 4u = 0$
  • $v^2$ terms: $v^2 - 2v^2 + 2v^2 = v^2$
  • $v$ terms: $4v - 8v + 8v - 4v = 0$
  • Constants: $4 - 8 + 8 - 8 = -4$

The equation simplifies to:

$$u^2 + v^2 - 4 = 45$$ $$u^2 + v^2 = 49$$

Why This Simplification is Remarkable!

The cross terms ($xy$) in the original equation made it a "rotated" ellipse. Our change of variables has "un-rotated" it! The result $u^2 + v^2 = 49$ is actually a circle of radius 7, which is a special case of an ellipse where both semi-axes are equal.

4
Compute the Jacobian

We need to find $\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|$. With $x = u - v - 2$ and $y = v + 2$:

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = (1)(1) - (-1)(0) = 1$$
5
Calculate the area

Since the Jacobian is 1, area is preserved under this transformation. The region in the $uv$-plane is a circle of radius 7.

$$\text{Area} = \iint_{u^2 + v^2 \leq 49} 1 \cdot |1|\, du\, dv = \pi r^2 = \pi(7)^2 = 49\pi$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{49\pi}$$

Practice Tips

  • When given a specific substitution, trust it - it's designed to simplify the problem!
  • Always expand carefully and track all cross terms
  • A Jacobian of 1 means area is preserved - the transformation is just a rotation/translation
  • Recognize standard forms: circles ($x^2 + y^2 = r^2$) and ellipses ($\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$)

Problem 3: Double Integral with Singularity

Problem Statement

Evaluate the integral $\displaystyle\iint_D \frac{1}{\sqrt{3 \cdot xy}}\, dA$, where $D = [0,8] \times [0,8]$

(Enter DNE if the integral does not converge or does not exist. Use symbolic notation and fractions where needed.)

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Improper integrals and convergence tests
  • Separable functions in double integrals
  • Recognizing singularities in integrands
  • Evaluating integrals with $x^{-1/2}$ terms

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Identify the singularity

The integrand $\frac{1}{\sqrt{3xy}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{y}}$ has singularities along $x = 0$ and $y = 0$.

This is an improper integral that requires careful analysis.

Why Might This Integral Converge?

Even though the integrand goes to infinity at the boundary, the singularity might be "weak" enough that the integral converges. For $\int_0^a \frac{1}{x^p} dx$, the integral converges if $p < 1$. Here, $p = 1/2 < 1$, so we expect convergence!

2
Separate the integral

Since the integrand factors as a product of functions of $x$ and $y$ only:

$$\iint_D \frac{1}{\sqrt{3xy}}\, dA = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \int_0^8 \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\, dx \cdot \int_0^8 \frac{1}{\sqrt{y}}\, dy$$
3
Evaluate each single integral

Both integrals are identical. Consider $\int_0^8 \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\, dx = \int_0^8 x^{-1/2}\, dx$:

$$\int_0^8 x^{-1/2}\, dx = \left[ \frac{x^{1/2}}{1/2} \right]_0^8 = \left[ 2\sqrt{x} \right]_0^8 = 2\sqrt{8} - 2\sqrt{0} = 2 \cdot 2\sqrt{2} = 4\sqrt{2}$$
4
Combine the results $$\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot (4\sqrt{2}) \cdot (4\sqrt{2}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot 16 \cdot 2 = \frac{32}{\sqrt{3}}$$

Rationalizing the denominator:

$$= \frac{32}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{32\sqrt{3}}{3}$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{32\sqrt{3}}{3}}$$

Key Integral Formula

$$\int x^{-1/2}\, dx = 2x^{1/2} + C = 2\sqrt{x} + C$$

More generally: $\int x^n\, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$ for $n \neq -1$

Practice Tips

  • Always check for singularities in the integrand before integrating
  • Remember: $\int_0^a x^{-p} dx$ converges if and only if $p < 1$
  • Separable integrands make double integrals into products of single integrals
  • Rationalize denominators when presenting final answers

Problem 4: Centroid of 3D Region

Problem Statement

Find the centroid of the region $W$ lying above the sphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 6$ and below the paraboloid $z = 4 - x^2 - y^2$. Assume the density $\delta(x,y,z) = 1$.

(Give your answer as point coordinates in the form (*,*,*).)

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Cylindrical coordinates $(r, \theta, z)$
  • Setting up triple integrals for volume and moments
  • Finding intersections of surfaces
  • Symmetry arguments for centroids

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Use symmetry to simplify

Both the sphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 6$ and the paraboloid $z = 4 - x^2 - y^2$ are symmetric about the $z$-axis. The region $W$ inherits this symmetry.

Therefore: $\bar{x} = 0$ and $\bar{y} = 0$

We only need to find $\bar{z}$.

Why Does Symmetry Work?

When a region is symmetric about the $z$-axis and has uniform density, every contribution to $\bar{x}$ from a point $(x,y,z)$ is cancelled by the point $(-x,-y,z)$. The same applies for $\bar{y}$.

2
Find where the surfaces intersect

The sphere: $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 6$, so $x^2 + y^2 = 6 - z^2$

The paraboloid: $z = 4 - x^2 - y^2$, so $x^2 + y^2 = 4 - z$

Setting these equal:

$$6 - z^2 = 4 - z$$ $$z^2 - z - 2 = 0$$ $$(z-2)(z+1) = 0$$

So $z = 2$ or $z = -1$

Since we're in the region above the sphere and below the paraboloid (which opens downward), the intersection occurs at $z = 2$ where $r^2 = x^2 + y^2 = 4 - 2 = 2$.

3
Set up cylindrical coordinates

Using $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $z = z$ with $dV = r\, dr\, d\theta\, dz$

The paraboloid: $z = 4 - r^2$

The sphere: $r^2 + z^2 = 6$, so $z = \sqrt{6 - r^2}$ (upper hemisphere)

For the region $W$: $z$ ranges from $\sqrt{6 - r^2}$ (sphere) to $4 - r^2$ (paraboloid)

The radial bound: $r$ ranges from $0$ to $\sqrt{2}$ (intersection circle)

4
Set up the volume integral $$V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} \int_{\sqrt{6-r^2}}^{4-r^2} r\, dz\, dr\, d\theta$$

Integrating with respect to $z$:

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r\left[(4-r^2) - \sqrt{6-r^2}\right] dr\, d\theta$$ $$= 2\pi \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} \left[4r - r^3 - r\sqrt{6-r^2}\right] dr$$
5
Evaluate the volume integral

Breaking into parts:

$$\int_0^{\sqrt{2}} 4r\, dr = \left[2r^2\right]_0^{\sqrt{2}} = 4$$ $$\int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r^3\, dr = \left[\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^{\sqrt{2}} = 1$$

For $\int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r\sqrt{6-r^2}\, dr$, use substitution $u = 6 - r^2$, $du = -2r\, dr$:

$$= -\frac{1}{2}\int_6^{4} \sqrt{u}\, du = \frac{1}{2}\int_4^{6} u^{1/2}\, du = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{2}{3}\left[u^{3/2}\right]_4^6 = \frac{1}{3}\left(6\sqrt{6} - 8\right)$$

Therefore:

$$V = 2\pi\left[4 - 1 - \frac{1}{3}(6\sqrt{6} - 8)\right] = 2\pi\left[3 - 2\sqrt{6} + \frac{8}{3}\right] = 2\pi\left[\frac{17}{3} - 2\sqrt{6}\right]$$
6
Set up and evaluate the moment integral $M_{xy}$ $$M_{xy} = \iiint_W z\, dV = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} \int_{\sqrt{6-r^2}}^{4-r^2} zr\, dz\, dr\, d\theta$$

Integrating with respect to $z$:

$$= 2\pi \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r \cdot \frac{1}{2}\left[(4-r^2)^2 - (6-r^2)\right] dr$$ $$= \pi \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r\left[(4-r^2)^2 - (6-r^2)\right] dr$$

Expanding $(4-r^2)^2 = 16 - 8r^2 + r^4$:

$$= \pi \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r\left[16 - 8r^2 + r^4 - 6 + r^2\right] dr$$ $$= \pi \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r\left[10 - 7r^2 + r^4\right] dr$$ $$= \pi \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} \left[10r - 7r^3 + r^5\right] dr$$

Evaluating:

$$= \pi\left[5r^2 - \frac{7r^4}{4} + \frac{r^6}{6}\right]_0^{\sqrt{2}}$$ $$= \pi\left[10 - 7 + \frac{8}{6}\right] = \pi\left[3 + \frac{4}{3}\right] = \frac{13\pi}{3}$$
7
Calculate $\bar{z}$ $$\bar{z} = \frac{M_{xy}}{V} = \frac{\frac{13\pi}{3}}{2\pi\left(\frac{17}{3} - 2\sqrt{6}\right)} = \frac{13}{6\left(\frac{17}{3} - 2\sqrt{6}\right)} = \frac{13}{34 - 12\sqrt{6}} \cdot \frac{34 + 12\sqrt{6}}{34 + 12\sqrt{6}}$$

After rationalization:

$$= \frac{13(34 + 12\sqrt{6})}{1156 - 864} = \frac{13(34 + 12\sqrt{6})}{292} = \frac{442 + 156\sqrt{6}}{292} = \frac{221 + 78\sqrt{6}}{146}$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{\left(0, 0, \frac{221 + 78\sqrt{6}}{146}\right)}$$

Numerically: approximately $(0, 0, 2.82)$

Centroid Formulas (Uniform Density)

$$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{V}\iiint_W x\, dV, \quad \bar{y} = \frac{1}{V}\iiint_W y\, dV, \quad \bar{z} = \frac{1}{V}\iiint_W z\, dV$$

Practice Tips

  • Always check for symmetry first - it can eliminate entire integrals!
  • Carefully determine which surface is "above" and which is "below"
  • Use cylindrical coordinates for problems with axial symmetry
  • Verify your bounds by checking intersection points

Problem 5: Line Integral (Vector Field)

Problem Statement

Compute $\displaystyle\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$, where $\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \left\langle \frac{-2y}{(x^2+y^2)^2}, \frac{2x}{(x^2+y^2)^2} \right\rangle$ and $C$ is a circle of radius 4 with center at the origin oriented counterclockwise.

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Parametrization of circles
  • Computing line integrals of vector fields
  • Recognizing exact/conservative vector fields
  • Green's Theorem and its limitations

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Parametrize the circle

A circle of radius 4 centered at origin, counterclockwise:

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle 4\cos t, 4\sin t \rangle, \quad 0 \leq t \leq 2\pi$$

The derivative:

$$\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle -4\sin t, 4\cos t \rangle$$
2
Evaluate $\mathbf{F}$ on the curve

On the circle, $x^2 + y^2 = 16$, so $(x^2 + y^2)^2 = 256$.

With $x = 4\cos t$ and $y = 4\sin t$:

$$\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \left\langle \frac{-2(4\sin t)}{256}, \frac{2(4\cos t)}{256} \right\rangle = \left\langle \frac{-\sin t}{32}, \frac{\cos t}{32} \right\rangle$$
3
Compute the dot product $\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)$ $$\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) = \left\langle \frac{-\sin t}{32}, \frac{\cos t}{32} \right\rangle \cdot \langle -4\sin t, 4\cos t \rangle$$ $$= \frac{-\sin t}{32} \cdot (-4\sin t) + \frac{\cos t}{32} \cdot (4\cos t)$$ $$= \frac{4\sin^2 t}{32} + \frac{4\cos^2 t}{32} = \frac{4(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t)}{32} = \frac{4}{32} = \frac{1}{8}$$

Why is the Dot Product Constant?

This is a beautiful result! The vector field $\mathbf{F}$ is related to the inverse-square law (like gravity or electrostatics). On circles centered at the singularity, the tangential component has constant magnitude. This is a deep property of such "radial" vector fields.

4
Evaluate the line integral $$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{8}\, dt = \frac{1}{8} \cdot 2\pi = \frac{\pi}{4}$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{\pi}{4}}$$

Why Can't We Use Green's Theorem?

Green's Theorem requires the vector field to be defined and have continuous partial derivatives on the entire region enclosed by the curve. Here, $\mathbf{F}$ is undefined at the origin (where $(x^2+y^2)^2 = 0$). Since the circle encloses the origin, Green's Theorem doesn't apply directly. We must use the parametric definition.

Practice Tips

  • Always check if the vector field has singularities inside the curve
  • Simplify $(x^2 + y^2)$ terms immediately when working on circles centered at origin
  • When the dot product is constant, the integral becomes trivial
  • Non-zero line integrals around closed curves indicate the field is not conservative

Problem 6: Line Integral (Scalar Function)

Problem Statement

Let $f(x,y,z) = 72xz - 8y^2$ and let $C$ be the curve given by $\mathbf{r}(t) = \left\langle t, \frac{t^2}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{t^3}{3} \right\rangle$ for $0 \leq t \leq 2$.

Compute $\displaystyle\int_C f\, ds$.

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Arc length parametrization: $ds = \|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|\, dt$
  • Computing derivatives of vector functions
  • Scalar line integrals vs. vector line integrals
  • Polynomial integration

Scalar Line Integral Formula

$$\int_C f\, ds = \int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|\, dt$$

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Compute $\mathbf{r}'(t)$

Given $\mathbf{r}(t) = \left\langle t, \frac{t^2}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{t^3}{3} \right\rangle$:

$$\mathbf{r}'(t) = \left\langle 1, \frac{2t}{\sqrt{2}}, t^2 \right\rangle = \left\langle 1, \sqrt{2}t, t^2 \right\rangle$$
2
Compute $\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|$ $$\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\| = \sqrt{1^2 + (\sqrt{2}t)^2 + (t^2)^2} = \sqrt{1 + 2t^2 + t^4}$$

Notice that $1 + 2t^2 + t^4 = (1 + t^2)^2$, so:

$$\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\| = \sqrt{(1 + t^2)^2} = 1 + t^2$$

(since $1 + t^2 > 0$ for all $t$)

Why This Simplification Matters

The curve was cleverly constructed so that $\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|$ becomes a perfect square. This is common in textbook problems and makes the integral tractable. In real applications, such simplifications are rare!

3
Evaluate $f(\mathbf{r}(t))$

Substitute $x = t$, $y = \frac{t^2}{\sqrt{2}}$, $z = \frac{t^3}{3}$ into $f(x,y,z) = 72xz - 8y^2$:

$$f(\mathbf{r}(t)) = 72 \cdot t \cdot \frac{t^3}{3} - 8\left(\frac{t^2}{\sqrt{2}}\right)^2$$ $$= 24t^4 - 8 \cdot \frac{t^4}{2} = 24t^4 - 4t^4 = 20t^4$$
4
Set up and evaluate the integral $$\int_C f\, ds = \int_0^2 f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|\, dt = \int_0^2 20t^4 \cdot (1 + t^2)\, dt$$

Expanding:

$$= \int_0^2 (20t^4 + 20t^6)\, dt = 20\int_0^2 (t^4 + t^6)\, dt$$

Integrating:

$$= 20\left[\frac{t^5}{5} + \frac{t^7}{7}\right]_0^2 = 20\left[\frac{32}{5} + \frac{128}{7}\right]$$ $$= 20 \cdot \frac{32 \cdot 7 + 128 \cdot 5}{35} = 20 \cdot \frac{224 + 640}{35} = 20 \cdot \frac{864}{35}$$ $$= \frac{17280}{35} = \frac{3456}{7}$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{3456}{7}}$$

Numerically: approximately $493.71$

Practice Tips

  • Always look for perfect squares in $\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|^2$ - factor before taking the square root
  • Simplify $f(\mathbf{r}(t))$ as much as possible before multiplying by $\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|$
  • Keep fractions throughout - don't convert to decimals until the very end
  • Double-check your power rule applications: $\int t^n\, dt = \frac{t^{n+1}}{n+1}$

Summary of Key Techniques

Problem Type Key Technique Critical Formula
Change of Variables (Double Integral) Compute Jacobian, transform bounds and integrand $\iint_D f\, dA = \iint_{D_0} f(G(u,v))|J|\, du\, dv$
Ellipse Area Transform to circle, use $\pi r^2$ Area of ellipse: $\pi ab$; Circle: $\pi r^2$
Improper Double Integrals Check convergence ($p < 1$), separate if possible $\int_0^a x^{-p}dx$ converges iff $p < 1$
Centroid (3D) Use symmetry, cylindrical coordinates $\bar{z} = \frac{1}{V}\iiint z\, dV$
Vector Line Integral Parametrize, compute $\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)$ $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)\, dt$
Scalar Line Integral Compute $\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|$, look for perfect squares $\int_C f\, ds = \int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t))\|\mathbf{r}'(t)\|\, dt$

General Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Identify the integral type: Double, triple, line (scalar or vector)?
  2. Check for symmetry: Can you immediately conclude some components are zero?
  3. Choose coordinates wisely: Cartesian, polar, cylindrical, or spherical?
  4. Look for simplifications: Perfect squares, separable integrands, constant values on curves
  5. Set up bounds carefully: Sketch the region, find intersections
  6. Verify your answer: Does the sign make sense? Is the magnitude reasonable?

Essential Jacobians to Memorize

Polar: $(x,y) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ $\Rightarrow$ $|J| = r$

Cylindrical: $(x,y,z) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, z)$ $\Rightarrow$ $|J| = r$

Spherical: $(x,y,z) = (\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \rho\cos\phi)$ $\Rightarrow$ $|J| = \rho^2\sin\phi$
