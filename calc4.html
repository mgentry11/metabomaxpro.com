Key Concepts Review

Surface Integrals (Flux)

A flux integral measures the flow of a vector field through a surface. We parametrize the surface, compute the normal vector via cross product, then integrate the dot product of the field with the normal.

Flux Integral Formula

$$\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(u,v)) \cdot (\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v)\, dA$$

Surface Area

For a parametric surface $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$, the surface area is found by integrating the magnitude of the cross product of partial derivatives over the parameter domain.

Surface Area Formula

$$S = \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|\, dA$$

Line Integrals in Differential Form

A line integral $\int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy + R\,dz$ is evaluated using a parametrization by converting each differential to its derivative times $dt$.

Differential Form Line Integral

$$\int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy + R\,dz = \int_a^b \left(P\frac{dx}{dt} + Q\frac{dy}{dt} + R\frac{dz}{dt}\right) dt$$

When Can't We Use Green's Theorem?

Green's Theorem requires the vector field to be defined and have continuous partial derivatives on the entire region enclosed by the curve. If there's a singularity inside the curve (like at the origin for inverse-square fields), we must compute the line integral directly using parametrization.

Problem 1: Surface Flux Integral (Cylinder)

Problem Statement

Let $\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = 2x\mathbf{i} - 2y\mathbf{j} + z^2\mathbf{k}$. Evaluate

$$\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

where $S$ is the cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = 4$ with $z \in [0,1]$.

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Parametrization of cylindrical surfaces
  • Computing normal vectors via cross products
  • Surface integrals of vector fields
  • Trigonometric identities (double angle formulas)

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Parametrize the cylindrical surface

The cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = 4$ has radius $r = 2$.

$$\mathbf{r}(\theta, z) = \langle 2\cos\theta, 2\sin\theta, z \rangle$$

with $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$ and $z \in [0, 1]$.

2
Compute partial derivatives $$\mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -2\sin\theta, 2\cos\theta, 0 \rangle$$ $$\mathbf{r}_z = \langle 0, 0, 1 \rangle$$
3
Compute the normal vector (cross product) $$\mathbf{r}_\theta \times \mathbf{r}_z = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ -2\sin\theta & 2\cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \langle 2\cos\theta, 2\sin\theta, 0 \rangle$$

This points outward from the cylinder (radially).

4
Evaluate $\mathbf{F}$ on the surface

Substituting $x = 2\cos\theta$, $y = 2\sin\theta$:

$$\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(\theta, z)) = \langle 4\cos\theta, -4\sin\theta, z^2 \rangle$$
5
Compute the dot product $$\mathbf{F} \cdot (\mathbf{r}_\theta \times \mathbf{r}_z) = (4\cos\theta)(2\cos\theta) + (-4\sin\theta)(2\sin\theta) + (z^2)(0)$$ $$= 8\cos^2\theta - 8\sin^2\theta = 8(\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta) = 8\cos(2\theta)$$
6
Evaluate the integral $$\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} 8\cos(2\theta)\, d\theta\, dz$$

Inner integral:

$$\int_0^{2\pi} 8\cos(2\theta)\, d\theta = 4\sin(2\theta)\Big|_0^{2\pi} = 4[\sin(4\pi) - \sin(0)] = 0$$

Why is the Flux Zero?

The vector field has perfect symmetry relative to the cylinder. The flux entering on one side exactly cancels the flux exiting on the opposite side. This is a consequence of the $\cos(2\theta)$ term integrating to zero over a full period.

Final Answer

$$\boxed{0}$$

Practice Tips

  • For cylinders, use $\mathbf{r}(\theta, z) = \langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, z \rangle$
  • The outward normal for a cylinder is always radial: $\langle \cos\theta, \sin\theta, 0 \rangle$ (scaled)
  • Look for trigonometric identities to simplify before integrating
  • $\int_0^{2\pi} \cos(n\theta)\, d\theta = 0$ for any integer $n \neq 0$

Problem 2: Surface Area of a Cone

Problem Statement

Find the surface area of the part of the cone $x^2 + y^2 = z^2$ between the planes $z = 3$ and $z = 8$.

(Express numbers in exact form. Use symbolic notation and fractions where needed.)

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Parametrization of cones
  • Computing cross products and their magnitudes
  • Surface area integrals
  • Understanding cone geometry

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Understand the cone equation

The equation $x^2 + y^2 = z^2$ describes a double cone. Since $z \in [3, 8]$ (positive), we're on the upper cone where $z = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = r$ in cylindrical coordinates.

2
Parametrize the cone surface

Using the constraint $z = r$:

$$\mathbf{r}(r, \theta) = \langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, r \rangle$$

with $r \in [3, 8]$ and $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$.

3
Compute partial derivatives $$\mathbf{r}_r = \langle \cos\theta, \sin\theta, 1 \rangle$$ $$\mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -r\sin\theta, r\cos\theta, 0 \rangle$$
4
Compute the cross product $$\mathbf{r}_r \times \mathbf{r}_\theta = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \cos\theta & \sin\theta & 1 \\ -r\sin\theta & r\cos\theta & 0 \end{vmatrix}$$ $$= \langle -r\cos\theta, -r\sin\theta, r \rangle$$
5
Find the magnitude $$|\mathbf{r}_r \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = \sqrt{r^2\cos^2\theta + r^2\sin^2\theta + r^2} = \sqrt{r^2 + r^2} = r\sqrt{2}$$

Why the Factor of $\sqrt{2}$?

The cone $z = r$ has a 45-degree slope. The slant height is always $\sqrt{2}$ times the vertical height (or radial distance). This geometric factor appears naturally in the surface area calculation.

6
Set up and evaluate the integral $$S = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_3^8 r\sqrt{2}\, dr\, d\theta = \sqrt{2} \int_0^{2\pi} d\theta \int_3^8 r\, dr$$ $$= \sqrt{2} \cdot 2\pi \cdot \left[\frac{r^2}{2}\right]_3^8 = \sqrt{2} \cdot 2\pi \cdot \frac{64 - 9}{2}$$ $$= \sqrt{2} \cdot 2\pi \cdot \frac{55}{2} = 55\pi\sqrt{2}$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{55\pi\sqrt{2}}$$

Practice Tips

  • For cones $z = r$, use $\mathbf{r}(r, \theta) = \langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, r \rangle$
  • The magnitude $|\mathbf{r}_r \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = r\sqrt{2}$ for 45-degree cones
  • Verify using frustum formula: $S = \pi(R_1 + R_2) \cdot s$ where $s$ is slant height

Problem 3: Line Integral (Differential Form)

Problem Statement

Evaluate the line integral

$$\int_C 9y\, dx + 8z\, dy + x\, dz$$

where $\mathbf{r}(t) = \left(2 + t^{-1}, t^3, t^2\right)$ for $0 \leq t \leq 1$.

(Give an exact answer. Use symbolic notation and fractions where needed.)

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Line integrals in differential form
  • Parametric curve derivatives
  • Improper integrals and convergence
  • Polynomial integration

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Extract components from parametrization

From $\mathbf{r}(t) = (2 + t^{-1}, t^3, t^2)$:

  • $x(t) = 2 + \frac{1}{t}$
  • $y(t) = t^3$
  • $z(t) = t^2$
2
Compute derivatives $$\frac{dx}{dt} = -\frac{1}{t^2}, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = 3t^2, \quad \frac{dz}{dt} = 2t$$
3
Substitute into the integral

The integral becomes:

$$\int_0^1 \left(9y \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} + 8z \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} + x \cdot \frac{dz}{dt}\right) dt$$

First term: $9y \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} = 9t^3 \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{t^2}\right) = -9t$

Second term: $8z \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} = 8t^2 \cdot 3t^2 = 24t^4$

Third term: $x \cdot \frac{dz}{dt} = \left(2 + \frac{1}{t}\right) \cdot 2t = 4t + 2$

4
Combine the integrand $$-9t + 24t^4 + 4t + 2 = 24t^4 - 5t + 2$$

Why Does This Integral Converge?

Although $t^{-1}$ is undefined at $t = 0$, the final integrand $24t^4 - 5t + 2$ is perfectly continuous on $[0, 1]$. The $t^{-1}$ term in $x(t)$ combined with $\frac{dx}{dt} = -t^{-2}$ produces $-9t$, which is well-behaved. The integral converges!

5
Evaluate the integral $$\int_0^1 (24t^4 - 5t + 2)\, dt = \left[\frac{24t^5}{5} - \frac{5t^2}{2} + 2t\right]_0^1$$ $$= \frac{24}{5} - \frac{5}{2} + 2 = \frac{48}{10} - \frac{25}{10} + \frac{20}{10} = \frac{43}{10}$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{43}{10}}$$

Numerically: 4.3

Practice Tips

  • Convert $P\,dx + Q\,dy + R\,dz$ to $\left(P\frac{dx}{dt} + Q\frac{dy}{dt} + R\frac{dz}{dt}\right)dt$
  • Simplify the integrand before integrating
  • Check for improper integrals when parametrization has singularities
  • Often, apparent singularities cancel out in the final integrand

Problem 4: Line Integral (Vector Field on Circle)

Problem Statement

Compute $\displaystyle\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$, where

$$\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \left\langle \frac{-6y}{(x^2+y^2)^2}, \frac{6x}{(x^2+y^2)^2} \right\rangle$$

and $C$ is a circle of radius 12 with center at the origin oriented counterclockwise.

(Use symbolic notation and fractions where needed.)

Prerequisite Knowledge

  • Parametrization of circles
  • Line integrals of vector fields
  • Recognizing singularities (why Green's Theorem doesn't apply)
  • Trigonometric simplification

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Identify the singularity

The vector field $\mathbf{F}$ has a singularity at the origin where $(x^2 + y^2)^2 = 0$. Since the origin lies inside the circle, Green's Theorem does not apply.

2
Parametrize the circle $$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle 12\cos t, 12\sin t \rangle, \quad t \in [0, 2\pi]$$ $$\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle -12\sin t, 12\cos t \rangle$$
3
Compute $x^2 + y^2$ on the curve

On the circle: $x^2 + y^2 = 144$, so $(x^2 + y^2)^2 = 20736$

4
Evaluate $\mathbf{F}$ on the curve $$\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \left\langle \frac{-6(12\sin t)}{20736}, \frac{6(12\cos t)}{20736} \right\rangle = \left\langle \frac{-\sin t}{288}, \frac{\cos t}{288} \right\rangle$$
5
Compute the dot product $$\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) = \frac{-\sin t}{288} \cdot (-12\sin t) + \frac{\cos t}{288} \cdot (12\cos t)$$ $$= \frac{12\sin^2 t}{288} + \frac{12\cos^2 t}{288} = \frac{12(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t)}{288} = \frac{12}{288} = \frac{1}{24}$$

Why is the Dot Product Constant?

This is a remarkable property of inverse-square type fields! On circles centered at the singularity, the tangential component has constant magnitude. The answer is independent of the radius - any circle around the origin gives the same result.

6
Evaluate the integral $$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{24}\, dt = \frac{1}{24} \cdot 2\pi = \frac{\pi}{12}$$

Final Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{\pi}{12}}$$

Practice Tips

  • Always check for singularities inside your curve before using Green's Theorem
  • On circles centered at origin: $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ (constant!)
  • When $\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)$ is constant, the integral is trivial
  • Non-zero line integral around closed curve $\Rightarrow$ field is NOT conservative

Summary of Key Techniques

Problem Type Key Technique Critical Formula
Flux Integral (Cylinder) Parametrize surface, compute $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$ $\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \mathbf{F} \cdot (\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v)\, dA$
Surface Area Parametrize surface, find $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|$ $S = \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|\, dA$
Line Integral (Differential) Substitute parametrization and derivatives $\int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy + R\,dz = \int (P\frac{dx}{dt} + Q\frac{dy}{dt} + R\frac{dz}{dt})\, dt$
Line Integral (Vector Field) Parametrize, compute $\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)$ $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)\, dt$

Common Surface Parametrizations

  • Cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$: $\mathbf{r}(\theta, z) = \langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, z \rangle$
  • Cone $z = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$: $\mathbf{r}(r, \theta) = \langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, r \rangle$
  • Sphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = R^2$: $\mathbf{r}(\phi, \theta) = \langle R\sin\phi\cos\theta, R\sin\phi\sin\theta, R\cos\phi \rangle$
  • Paraboloid $z = x^2 + y^2$: $\mathbf{r}(r, \theta) = \langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, r^2 \rangle$

When to Use What Theorem

Green's Theorem: Line integral $\leftrightarrow$ Double integral (2D, no singularities inside)

Stokes' Theorem: Line integral $\leftrightarrow$ Surface integral (3D)

Divergence Theorem: Surface integral $\leftrightarrow$ Triple integral (closed surfaces)
